Lesson 1 · Early India: From the Beginnings to the Indus Civilisation

Timeframe · c. 2 million BCE – 1300 BCE Archaeological sources (lithic assemblages, faunal/floral remains, palaeo-DNA) stitched with settlement evidence track the transition from hunter-gatherers to the Bronze Age urbanism of the Indus.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 2 million – 60,000 BCE · Lower Palaeolithic
    Between roughly 2 million and 60,000 BCE, Homo erectus foragers camped along the Narmada valley and peninsular plateau sites like Athirampakkam, Hunsgi and Isampur, shaping Acheulian hand-axes and cleavers to quarter megafauna, showing how early Indians adapted toolkits to open savannah ecologies.
  2. c. 385,000 – 40,000 BCE · Middle Palaeolithic
    From c. 385,000–40,000 BCE, archaic Homo sapiens communities across the Narmada, Son, Godavari and Krishna basins prepared stone cores and struck elongated flakes and scrapers to process hides, illustrating when and how cognitive planning and mobility networks intensified in subcontinental interiors.
  3. c. 40,000 – 10,000 BCE · Upper Palaeolithic
    During 40,000–10,000 BCE, modern humans occupied Patne, Bhimbetka and Sri Lankan caves, producing blade-microlith toolkits, ostrich eggshell beads and rock art panels, evidence that symbolic behaviour spread across western and central India as people tracked migratory herds at the close of the Ice Age.
  4. c. 10,000 – 6000 BCE · Mesolithic
    With post-glacial warming between 10,000 and 6000 BCE, microlith-using hunter-gatherers at Bagor, Langhnaj, Adamgarh and Sarai Nahar Rai shifted to seasonal camps, tamed dogs and early cattle, and painted hunt-ritual scenes, showing why semi-sedentary lifeways emerged around reliable lakes and grasslands.
  5. c. 7000 – 5500 BCE · Neolithic Transition
    By c. 7000–5500 BCE, aceramic farmers at Mehrgarh in Baluchistan and sites like Burzahom, Koldihwa, Chirand and Hallur domesticated barley, wheat and zebu cattle, dug storage pits and used ground-stone axes, revealing where and how food production took root before radiating to other Indian eco-zones.
  6. c. 3300 – 2600 BCE · Early Harappan
    Between 3300 and 2600 BCE, early Harappan villages at Kot Diji, Kalibangan and Banawali ringed their habitation mounds with mudbrick walls, laid out drains and painted red-black pottery, signalling why agro-pastoral chiefs experimented with proto-urban planning along the Indus and Ghaggar-Hakra systems.
  7. c. 2600 – 1900 BCE · Mature Indus Civilisation
    From 2600 to 1900 BCE, cities such as Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, Dholavira and Lothal used baked-brick grids, standardized weights, dockyards and craft quarters, enabling merchants to ship beads and cotton from Gujarat to Mesopotamia—explaining how riverine basins hosted one of the world’s earliest urban networks.
  8. c. 1900 – 1300 BCE · Late Harappan Transformations
    After 1900 BCE, Harappan populations dispersed toward the upper Ganga and Rajasthan plains, reusing painted wares (Jhukar, Rangpur), mixing farming with herding and creating smaller fortified hamlets, which shows when and why ecological stress and shifting trade routes fragmented the Bronze Age metropolis.

Source & Method Anchors

  • Archaeology dominates; absence of deciphered script demands multi-proxy data (lithics, sedimentology, charred seeds, phytoliths, faunal remains).
  • Mitochondrial DNA and attempts at ancient DNA extraction inform migration hypotheses.
  • Linguistic layering of Indo-Aryan, Dravidian, Austroasiatic and Tibeto-Burman families aligns with phased migrations.

Society & Economy

  • Palaeolithic hunter-gatherers exploit riverine ecotones, use seasonal mobility, and inhabit caves (Bhimbetka) and open-air sites.
  • Neolithic agro-pastoral life introduces domesticated cattle, sheep, goats, buffaloes; storage silos and wattle-and-daub houses emerge.
  • Indus urbanism sustains irrigated cereal agriculture (wheat, barley), cotton cultivation, and a redistributive craft economy with specialised artisans.

Technology & Culture

  • Palaeolithic cognitive skills evidenced via symmetry in hand-axe design and controlled percussion.
  • Upper Palaeolithic ornamental culture (shell/stone beads) and rock art foreshadow ritualisation.
  • Indus innovations include bronze metallurgy, faience, steatite seals with iconography (unicorn, proto-Shiva), dockyard at Lothal, and water management at Dholavira.

Belief & Ideology

  • Animistic and totemic tendencies inferred from rock art and burial goods.
  • Indus religion emphasises fertility cults (Mother Goddess figurines), animal symbolism (bull, elephant), and sacred groves; fire altars at Kalibangan hint at ritual continuity.

Trans-Regional Links

  • Parallel Neolithic revolutions in Fertile Crescent, Indus, Ganges valley and China (10,000–5000 BCE) underscore shared agro-revolution timeframe.
  • Indus maritime trade connects to Mesopotamia (Meluhha ports), Bahrain (Dilmun) and Oman (Magan); carnelian beads and seals recovered at Ur and Susa.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Chronology of Stone Age phases and diagnostic tool types.
  • Unique features by key sites (Rakhigarhi's granaries, Dholavira's water reservoirs, Lothal's dock).
  • Debates on Indus decline (tectonics, river shift, climate aridification) with material evidence.
Critic's Calibration: Keep Lower–Middle–Upper Palaeolithic boundaries precise; tie each cultural phase to sites and techno-traits. Note that undeciphered script complicates social stratification debates—highlight archaeological proxies (housing tiers, craft quarters) instead of speculative caste parallels.
  • Cross-referenced lithic industries with their respective geographies for prelims map questions.
  • Integrated Indus weight system (13.63 g) and craft evidence to handle mains economy questions.
  • Flagged continuity from Late Harappan to Ganga plain Chalcolithic villages for culture-change essays.

Lesson 2 · Early India: Chalcolithic, Megalithic, Iron Age & Vedic Cultures

Timeframe · c. 3000 – 600 BCE Diverse agro-pastoral communities, copper/iron transitions, and Vedic textual traditions overlay to usher early historic foundations.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 3000 – 1900 BCE · Late Harappan & Pre-Aryan Settlements
    Between 3000 and 1900 BCE, rural Chalcolithic hamlets with wattle-and-daub houses, silos and copper hoards thrived alongside fading Indus centres in Haryana, Rajasthan and the Upper Ganga plain, indicating how farmers sustained mixed cropping even as urban cores declined.
  2. c. 2600 – 1200 BCE · Ochre Coloured Pottery (OCP)
    From c. 2600–1200 BCE, Ochre Coloured Pottery communities stretched from Badaun to Hastinapur, burying copper hoards under red-slipped jars while grazing cattle and sowing rice-barley, suggesting why proto-Vedic clans occupied the doab before Mahajanapada polities formed.
  3. c. 2400 – 1700 BCE · Ahar-Banas & Malwa Traditions
    Across 2400–1700 BCE in Rajasthan’s Ahar-Banas and Malwa plateau sites such as Balathal, Gilund and Navdatoli, farmers lived in mud-brick houses, fired black-and-red ware and smelted copper to support millet-horticulture economies suited to the Aravalli foothills.
  4. c. 2000 – 1000 BCE · Deccan & South Indian Chalcolithic
    Between 2000 and 1000 BCE, Jorwe, Daimabad and southern Neolithic-Chalcolithic villages combined stone-copper tools, granaries and cattle pens on the Deccan black soils, showing where dry farming of millets and pulses underpinned emerging craft specialisation.
  5. c. 1800 – 800 BCE · Megalithic Burials (Tamilakam & Deccan)
    From 1800 to 800 BCE, chiefs across Tamilakam and the Deccan marked authority through cist and dolmen burials at Adichanallur, Brahmagiri and Maski, interring iron weapons, black ware and carnelian beads, which explains how ancestor cults legitimised warrior lineages before the Sangam age.
  6. c. 1100 – 800 BCE · Painted Grey Ware (PGW) & Iron Introduction
    During 1100–800 BCE, Painted Grey Ware villages appeared along the Ganga-Yamuna doab—sites like Hastinapur and Atranjikhera using iron ploughs, nucleated layouts and tribal assemblies—revealing when iron technology accelerated agrarian surplus for Kuru-Panchala polities.
  7. c. 1000 – 600 BCE · Early & Later Vedic Phases
    Between 1000 and 600 BCE, Rig Vedic pastoral clans occupying Punjab transitioned into settled monarchies in the Middle Ganga valley, codifying varna hierarchies, elaborate yajnas and sabha-samiti institutions, clarifying why a new socio-political order preceded the Mahajanapadas.

Textual & Archaeological Correlation

  • Rigveda (Books 2–7) reflects early semi-nomadic clans (Bharata, Purus) on Sarasvati-Sutlej; later books and Brahmanas map onto eastward shifts.
  • Brahmanas, Aranyakas, Upanishads elucidate ritual-philosophical layers; Sama Veda codifies musical liturgy, Atharva Veda preserves charms.
  • Zend Avesta parallels reinforce Indo-Iranian linguistic continuum, hinting at external homelands.

Socio-Political Evolution

  • Early Vedic polity: tribal assemblies (Sabha, Samiti), rajan as primus inter pares; cattle wealth equals status.
  • Later Vedic shift: hereditary monarchy, emergence of Mahajanapadas, codified taxation (bali, bhaga) and standing armies.
  • Varna stratification crystallises; hereditary priesthood (Brahmins), warrior nobility (Kshatriyas), agriculturist Vis, and marginalised Shudras.

Economy & Technology

  • Chalcolithic economies mix subsistence farming with cattle pastoralism; copper, stone, terracotta artefacts illustrate craft diversity.
  • Iron technology (from c. 1100 BCE) boosts plough agriculture, forest clearance, and surplus, enabling nucleated settlements.
  • Use of silos, granaries, bullock-drawn ploughs; emergence of secondary craft specialisation (metallurgy, pottery firing).

Religion & Thought

  • Vedic pantheon: Indra (war/rain), Agni (fire), Varuna (cosmic order), Ushas (dawn); rituals centre on soma, yajnas, dana.
  • Philosophical transition from ritual (Brahmana) to speculative monism (Upanishads: atman-brahman unity, karma).
  • Non-Vedic cults (pre-Aryan fertility, serpent worship) persist and later syncretise with Vedic practice.

Peninsula Highlights

  • Megalithic Tamil sites yield black-ware, iron weapons, gold beads; Brahmi inscriptions at Kodumanal show early script adoption.
  • Subsistence based on millets, pulses, tuber collection; evidence of cattle pens, horse remains signalling mobility.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Chalcolithic traditions (Ahar, Malwa, Jorwe) by pottery motifs and staple crops.
  • Contrast Early vs Later Vedic polity, economy, gender roles (decline of women's ritual access).
  • Map distribution of Megalithic burials for prelims geography; link to iron spread.
Critic's Calibration: Stress that correlating Vedic textual horizons with specific archaeological cultures remains debated—present multiple scholarly views when answering mains. Mention that Vedic texts were orally transmitted till late first millennium CE to avoid anachronistic literacy claims.
  • Timeline interlocks Chalcolithic sequences with Vedic textual phases for integrated essays.
  • Captured southern Megalithic features (urn burials, hero-stone lore) often ignored by northern-centric prep.
  • Flagged Indo-Iranian linguistic links (Zend Avesta) for culture-contact questions.

Lesson 3 · Rise of Territorial Kingdoms & Heterodox Movements

Timeframe · c. 600 – 300 BCE Second urbanisation in the Gangetic basin births mahajanapadas and sparks philosophical churn (Buddhism, Jainism, Ajivikas) challenging orthodox Vedic order.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 600 BCE · Consolidation of Janapadas
    Around 600 BCE, sixteen Mahajanapadas from Gandhara to Magadha fortified capitals like Rajagriha and Ujjain, minted punch-marked coins and levied taxes, illustrating when tribal republics crystallised into territorial states to control trade routes.
  2. 6th century BCE · Second Urbanisation
    In the 6th century BCE, iron ploughs, craft guilds and caravan roads (Uttarapatha, Dakshinapatha) fostered new towns such as Vaishali and Sravasti, showing how agrarian surplus underpinned a second wave of urbanism across the Middle Ganga plain.
  3. 599 – 527 BCE · Mahavira & Jain Sangha
    Between 599 and 527 BCE, Vardhamana Mahavira travelled through Bihar’s republican centres preaching ahimsa, aparigraha and the triratna to merchant-urban audiences, explaining why Jain sanghas flourished where trade and renunciation intersected.
  4. 563 – 483 BCE · Life of Gautama Buddha
    From 563 to 483 BCE, Siddhartha of the Sakya clan left Kapilavastu, attained enlightenment at Bodh Gaya, taught the Middle Path at Sarnath and built monastic networks across Kosala and Magadha, revealing how a prince-turned-monk articulated solutions to samsara.
  5. 5th – 3rd centuries BCE · Buddhist Councils & Spread
    Between the 5th and 3rd centuries BCE, councils at Rajagriha, Vaishali, Pataliputra and later Kundalavana debated doctrine and dispatched missionaries from Ashoka’s court to Sri Lanka, Central Asia and China, clarifying how Buddhist canons and sects evolved.
  6. 6th – 4th centuries BCE · Ajivika Ascendancy
    During the 6th–4th centuries BCE, Makkhali Gosala’s Ajivikas, backed by Mauryan patrons in Magadha and Tamil polities, preached niyati (fatalism) from rock-cut caves such as Barabar, indicating why rival Shramana orders competed for royal legitimacy.

Polity & Economy

  • Mahajanapadas adopt standing armies, forts (rajagriha cyclopean walls), and bureaucracy; Magadha leverages iron-rich Rajgir-Chotanagpur belt.
  • Cities host craft guilds (srenis), merchants cultivate caravan trade, while land revenue and customs underpin treasury.
  • Ajatashatru's chariot warfare and fortified capital shift illustrate military innovation.

Social Texture

  • Urbanisation fosters varna mobility but also social anxieties prompting sramana movements.
  • Samgama (assemblies) and gana-sangha republics (e.g., Vajji confederacy) coexist with monarchies, offering models of collective governance.
  • Merchants and artisans gain prominence via nigama and nigama-sabha networks.

Philosophical Currents

  • Buddha's Four Noble Truths and Eightfold Path reject ritualism, emphasise middle path and scepticism on metaphysical speculation.
  • Mahavira's five vows (ahimsa, satya, asteya, brahmacharya, aparigraha) and anekantavada (multiplicity of truths) lay ethical framework.
  • Ajivika determinism (niyati) posits predestined cycles; rival texts often caricature them, requiring balanced interpretation.

Institutional Building

  • Buddhist Sangha organised with bhikkhu/bhikkhuni ordination, disciplined via Vinaya; monasteries become urban welfare hubs.
  • Jain monastic codes differentiate between ascetics and lay shravakas; Jain councils compile Agamas.
  • Ajivika sect operates communal monasteries; receives cave grants in Barabar (Ashokan inscriptions).

Trans-Regional Impact

  • Buddhism travels to Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Central Asia, China (Hieun Tsang later records doctrinal debates).
  • Jainism spreads to western/southern India; texts translated into Prakrit and Tamil (e.g., Jivaka Chintamani).
  • Mahajanapada trade links extend towards Persia and along Himalayan passes, exchanging horses, textiles, precious stones.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate gana-sangha vs monarchy features with textual citations (Mahaparinibbana Sutta for Vajji constitution).
  • Memorise chronology of Buddhist councils and patron kings.
  • Use Ajivika references in Sangam literature for essay nuance on lesser-known sects.
Critic's Calibration: Avoid homogenising "shramana" schools—highlight doctrinal distinctions and socio-economic bases. Include Ajivika taxation under Cholas to demonstrate continuity into medieval south, a commonly missed prelims nugget.
  • Second urbanisation drivers linked to iron technology and trade corridors.
  • Embedded textual references (Samannaphala Sutta) for authenticity in mains answers.
  • Balanced treatment of Jain schism (Svetambara/Digambara) with implications for iconography.

Lesson 4 · State and Empire: Magadha to the Mauryas

Timeframe · c. 6th – 2nd century BCE Magadha's ascendancy culminates in the Mauryan Empire, synthesising administrative sophistication, imperial ideology and dhamma-based diplomacy.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 544 – 492 BCE · Bimbisara's Haryanka Rule
    Between 544 and 492 BCE, King Bimbisara of Magadha married Lichchavi and Kosalan princesses, fortified Rajagriha and created a spy network, demonstrating how diplomacy and intelligence expanded eastern India’s first great monarchy.
  2. c. 492 – 460 BCE · Ajatashatru
    From 492 to 460 BCE, Ajatashatru waged war on Kosala and the Vajji confederacy with engines like rathamusala, founded Pataliputra at the Ganga-Son junction and reorganised administration, explaining why Magadha eclipsed rival janapadas.
  3. c. 4th century BCE · Nandas & Imperial Resources
    In the 4th century BCE, Mahapadma Nanda amassed immense infantry, cavalry and war elephants funded by tight taxation across the middle Ganga basin, underscoring how centralised revenue enabled Magadha to deter foreign invaders.
  4. 326 BCE · Alexander's Invasion
    In 326 BCE, Alexander crossed the Indus, fought Porus at Hydaspes and halted at the Hyphasis when Macedonian troops refused to march into Magadha, creating overland contacts that later brought Megasthenes to Chandragupta’s court.
  5. 321 – 297 BCE · Chandragupta Maurya
    Between 321 and 297 BCE, Chandragupta Maurya overthrew the Nandas, seized Pataliputra, defeated Seleucus Nicator to annex Kabul and Baluchistan, and issued punch-marked coins, marking when the Mauryan Empire unified northern India.
  6. 297 – 268 BCE · Bindusara
    From 297 to 268 BCE, Bindusara governed from Ujjain to Mysore, maintaining diplomatic ties with Syrian and Egyptian courts through envoys like Deimachus, showing how Mauryan control stretched deep into the Deccan before Ashoka.
  7. 268 – 232 BCE · Ashoka the Great
    Between 268 and 232 BCE, Ashoka conquered Kalinga from his capital at Pataliputra, embraced Dhamma, carved rock and pillar edicts across South Asia and dispatched emissaries to Sri Lanka, Alexandria and Antioch, revealing how remorse reshaped imperial policy.
  8. c. 232 – 185 BCE · Mauryan Decline
    After Ashoka’s death c. 232 BCE, weak successors like Dasharatha and Brihadratha lost frontier satrapies to the Shungas and Indo-Greeks, while provincial governors asserted autonomy, explaining why the Mauryan political order unraveled by 185 BCE.

Administration & Governance

  • Centralised monarchy supported by Mantriparishad; provinces (mahalas) under kumara viceroys, districts (aharas) under amatyas, villages under gramikas.
  • Arthashastra outlines espionage (gudhapurusha), fiscal boards (samaharta, sannidhata) and detailed regulation of weights, measures, trade.
  • Ashoka appoints dhamma-mahamattas, pradesikas, rajukas for moral and judicial oversight.

Economy & Trade

  • State monopolies on mining, salt, forests; irrigation (Sudarsana lake) supports agrarian surplus.
  • Craft guilds taxed; textiles, woollens, silks, aromatics traded with Sri Lanka, China, and Hellenistic west via ports like Tamralipti.
  • Standardised punch-marked coinage facilitates long-distance commerce.

Society & Culture

  • Varna order acknowledged but with royal welfare focus; edicts emphasise respect for Brahmins, Shramanas, elders, servants.
  • Urban centres (Pataliputra) showcase wooden palisades, pillared halls (as described by Megasthenes).
  • Palace art influenced by Achaemenid techniques (polished sandstone, lotus-capitals).

Dhamma & Diplomacy

  • Ashoka's Dhamma integrates ahimsa, compassionate governance, inter-sect tolerance, prohibition of animal sacrifice.
  • Missionary outreach: Mahinda & Sangamitta to Sri Lanka; envoys to Syria (Antiochus II), Egypt (Ptolemy II), Macedonia (Antigonus).
  • Emphasis on welfare measures: dharmashalas, wells, banyan planting, medical aid for humans and animals.

Military & Frontiers

  • Huge standing army (as per Greek sources) with infantry, cavalry, war elephants, chariots.
  • Frontier policy includes marriage alliance with Seleucus (Chandragupta marries his daughter), garrison towns in northwest.
  • Post-Ashoka fragmentation sees Greek, Shaka inroads in northwest.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Distinguish Arthashastra's prescriptive statecraft from Ashokan ethical governance.
  • Locate Major Rock Edicts and Pillar Edicts for map questions.
  • Discuss Mauryan art (Sarnath lion capital, stupas) as state ideology carriers.
Critic's Calibration: Highlight debate on Mauryan centralisation vs segmentary governance; cite edict evidence when asserting welfare measures. Mention that trade links extended simultaneously to Sri Lanka and China to underscore pan-Asian exchange.
  • Sequenced Magadhan dynasties leading to Mauryas for continuity.
  • Integrated Arthashastra prescriptions with edictal evidence to avoid one-source bias.
  • Noted international diplomacy (Hellenistic ties) often omitted in quick summaries.

Lesson 5 · Society in South India (Satavahanas & Sangam Polities)

Timeframe · c. 3rd century BCE – 3rd century CE Post-Mauryan Deccan and Tamilakam witness agrarian expansion, mercantile guild power, and vibrant literary cultures under Satavahanas, Chera-Chola-Pandya realms.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 3rd – 2nd century BCE · Early Chiefdoms
    During the 3rd–2nd centuries BCE, post-Mauryan chieftains in the Deccan and Andhra minted Prakrit coins and issued donative inscriptions at sites like Amaravati, foreshadowing how regional houses such as the Satavahanas would emerge.
  2. c. 1st century BCE · Rise of Satavahanas
    In the 1st century BCE, Simuka and Satakarni rulers consolidated the upper Godavari basin from capitals at Pratishthana and Dhanyakataka, fielding elephant corps and granting land to Brahmanas, signalling why a native Deccan empire formed.
  3. 106 – 130 CE · Gautamiputra Satakarni
    Between 106 and 130 CE, Gautamiputra Satakarni defeated Western Kshatrapa Nahapana near Nashik, restruck his silver coins and styled himself trisasthipati, demonstrating how he protected Deccan trade corridors for Brahmana and Buddhist beneficiaries.
  4. 130 – 160 CE · Vasishthiputra Pulumavi & Yajna Sri Satakarni
    From 130 to 160 CE, Vasishthiputra Pulumavi and Yajna Sri Satakarni stabilised maritime exchange with Roman Egypt via ports like Kalyan and Sopara, issued bilingual coins and sponsored Amaravati’s sculptural programme, showing how coastal control funded the state.
  5. c. 300 BCE – 300 CE · Sangam Age in Tamilakam
    Across roughly 300 BCE–300 CE, the Chera, Chola and Pandya rulers headquartered at Karur, Puhar and Madurai patronised Sangam assemblies that compiled Ettuthogai and Pattuppattu poems, explaining why bardic networks preserved Tamil society’s landscape divisions.
  6. c. 3rd century CE · Decline & Successors
    By the 3rd century CE, Satavahana authority declined under Ikshvaku, Pallava and Kadamba rivals while Tamilakam saw the disruptive Kalabhra interregnum, highlighting how shifting trade winds and feudatories fractured earlier dynasties.

Primary Sources

  • Inscriptions: Nasik, Karle, Nanaghat (Prakrit) record royal charity, tax remission.
  • Literature: Gatha Saptasati (Hala), Sangam corpus (Aganaanuru, Purananuru) reveal social ethos.
  • Numismatics: Portrait coins with prashasti legends, regional coinages (Cheras' bow and arrow, Pandyas' fish).

Society & Stratification

  • Satavahana society blends Brahmanical norms (varna references, gotra) with matrilineal hints (queens' lineage in inscriptions).
  • Sangam society structured around tinai (ecozones), valued martial heroism (puram) and love ethics (agam); velir chieftains and vendars interact with gana-based communities.
  • Women poets like Avvaiyar, Kaakkaipadiniyar highlight female literacy and patronage.

Economy & Trade

  • Land grants (brahmadeya, agrahara) proliferate; irrigation tanks and canals expand cultivation.
  • Ports (Sopara, Kalyan, Muziris, Arikamedu) connect to Mediterranean, Southeast Asia, China; trade items include spices, pearls, cotton, precious stones.
  • Guilds (Nigamas, Manigramam, Ayyavole) regulate craft/trade; inscriptions record tax exemptions to guilds supporting stupas and temples.

Religion & Culture

  • Buddhism flourishes (Amaravati stupas, Nagarjunakonda Viharas) alongside flourishing Brahmanism (Vedic sacrifices, Agrahara grants) and Jainism (Mangulam, Jambai inscriptions).
  • Sangam literature captures hero-stones (nadukal) commemorating fallen warriors; patronage for Tamil grammar (Tolkappiyam) and music (Paanar tradition).
  • Satavahana art depicts yakshis, lotus medallions, narrative reliefs anticipating later Amaravati school.

Political Structure

  • Satavahanas adopt feudatory system with maharathis, mahabhojas; queens appear as regents (e.g., Naganika at Nanaghat).
  • Chola/Chera/Pandya kings styled as Vendar; local assemblies (ur, sabha, nagaram) manage irrigation, justice.
  • Military relies on elephant corps, cavalry; maritime navy ensures control of coastal trade.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Note Satavahana practice of matronymic gotra (Gautami-putra) for gender discourse questions.
  • Use Sangam tinai ecology to connect environment with culture.
  • Cite Nanaghat inscription for early tax exemption evidence (proto fiscal policy).
Critic's Calibration: Balance Buddhist evidence from Andhra with Brahmanical resurgence; highlight that trade links reach China (Chinese geographers note elephant corps) to satisfy global-interaction angle.
  • Integrated guild, land-grant and port data for economy answers.
  • Included women poets and matrilineal hints to avoid androcentric narrative.
  • Flagged continuation into Kalabhra/Pallava transitions for continuity questions.

Lesson 6 · Post-Mauryan Polities & Cross-Cultural Currents

Timeframe · c. 2nd century BCE – 3rd century CE Indo-Greeks, Shakas, Parthians and Kushans reshape north-western India, stimulating trans-Eurasian trade, artistic syncretism and Buddhist expansion towards China.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 200 – 100 BCE · Indo-Greek (Yavana) Rule
    Between 200 and 100 BCE, Indo-Greek kings Demetrius and Menander ruled from Taxila and Sakala, blending Hellenistic iconography with Indian motifs, debating monks like Nagasena and minting bilingual coins to secure the Kabul–Punjab trade artery.
  2. 1st century BCE · Shaka-Kshatrapa Ascendancy
    In the 1st century BCE, Shaka satraps such as Nahapana displaced the Indo-Greeks around Bharuch and Ujjain, taxing caravan routes that linked the Deccan to the Arabian Sea, which explains why western India shows hybrid Greek-Iranian art.
  3. c. 50 BCE – 50 CE · Indo-Parthians & Satrap Integration
    From 50 BCE to 50 CE, Indo-Parthian rulers like Gondophernes married into local elites, issued square copper coins and hosted traditions about St. Thomas at Taxila, showing how Iranian merchants stitched together Sindh and Gandhara markets.
  4. c. 45 – 78 CE · Kushan Consolidation
    Between c. 45 and 78 CE, Kujula Kadphises united the Yuezhi clans in Bactria, crossed the Hindu Kush and seized Peshawar, inaugurating the Kushan era that rerouted silk and spice caravans toward Central Asia and Rome.
  5. c. 127 – 150 CE · Kanishka I's Zenith
    During 127–150 CE, Kanishka I ruled from Purushapura, convened the Fourth Buddhist Council at Kundalvana, spread Mahayana doctrines to China and sponsored Gandhara-Mathura art, marking when imperial patronage globalised Buddhism.
  6. c. 200 – 300 CE · Kushan Decline & Satrap Autonomy
    By 200–300 CE, Kushan power waned under Sasanian pressure and Gupta resurgence, allowing local republics such as the Yaudheyas and Nagas to issue their own coins, illustrating how frontier satrapies reclaimed autonomy.

Political & Administrative Traits

  • Satrapal governance blends Hellenistic titles (strategos) with Indian kshatrapa designations; coins trilingual (Greek, Kharosthi, Brahmi).
  • Kushan kings adopt divine kingship (Shaonanoshao), maintain provincial satraps, control Silk Road nodes (Taxila, Begram).
  • Military strength rests on cavalry (Central Asian horse archers) and war elephants.

Trade & Economy

  • Silk Road traffic intensifies; merchants become intermediaries between China and Roman Empire, trading silk, spices, precious stones, glassware.
  • Karakoram passes facilitate missionary and commercial movement; modern Karakoram highway excavations unearth Buddhist inscriptions along routes.
  • Port trade shifts to Coromandel as overland routes face instability; silk cloth re-routed by sea to southern ports.

Religion & Culture

  • Gandhara art fuses Greco-Roman naturalism with Buddhist themes (anthropomorphic Buddha, draped robes), while Mathura style emphasises indigenous motifs.
  • Kanishka patronises Mahayana Buddhism, yet coins depict diverse deities (Buddha, Nana, Oesho), reflecting syncretism.
  • Buddhist missions travel to China; monasteries along trade routes host pilgrims and merchants.

Society & Urbanism

  • Urban centres (Taxila, Mathura, Ujjain) thrive with multi-ethnic populations (Indians, Greeks, Parthians, Central Asians).
  • Guilds accumulate wealth, finance stupas (Sanchi, Amaravati) and temples; women donors appear in inscriptions.
  • Cultural exchanges introduce Central Asian dressing, coin portraiture, and new scripts (Kharosthi demise by 4th century).

Trans-Regional Diplomacy

  • Indo-Roman trade recorded in Periplus; Roman gold coins found in south India evidence balance of trade favouring India.
  • Embassies between Kushans and Chinese Han court; Chinese chronicles note Yuezhi (Kui-shuang) rule.
  • Interplay with Parthians influences coin imagery and titles.

Lesson 7 · The Gupta Age

Timeframe · c. 240 – 550 CE Era of imperial resurgence melding administrative efficiency, classical art, scientific advances, and expansive trade networks linking India to China and the Mediterranean.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 240 CE · Gupta Foundations
    Around 240 CE, Sri Gupta and successor Ghatotkacha served as Magadhan feudatories, endowing temples near Prayaga and forging alliances that prepared the stage for a north Indian imperial revival.
  2. 319/20 CE · Chandragupta I
    In 319/20 CE, Chandragupta I adopted the title Maharajadhiraja after marrying Licchavi princess Kumaradevi, uniting Ganga heartland territories from Pataliputra, which set the Gupta era chronology.
  3. 335 – 375 CE · Samudragupta
    Between 335 and 375 CE, Samudragupta’s Allahabad pillar inscription recorded his digvijaya across Aryavarta and Dakshinapatha, detailing how he subjugated forest tribes, coastal ports and southern monarchs while sponsoring music and Brahmanical sacrifices.
  4. 375 – 415 CE · Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya)
    From 375 to 415 CE, Chandragupta II ousted the Western Kshatrapas, shifted a second capital to Ujjain, hosted luminaries like Kalidasa and welcomed Chinese pilgrim Faxian, demonstrating why Gupta rule symbolises a classical cultural apex.
  5. 415 – 455 CE · Kumaragupta I
    Between 415 and 455 CE, Kumaragupta I maintained empire-wide stability, founded Nalanda Mahavihara on the Ganga plain and struck Skanda-type coins to celebrate martial prowess, revealing how education and warfare intertwined.
  6. 455 – 467 CE · Skandagupta
    During 455–467 CE, Skandagupta repelled initial Huna invasions near Mathura and rebuilt collapsed embankments, but ongoing frontier wars drained the treasury, foreshadowing the fragmentation of Gupta authority by the mid-6th century.

Administration & Society

  • Empire divided into bhuktis (provinces) under uparikas, vishayas under vishyapatis, villages under gramikas; local bodies (kumaramatyas) inherited from Mauryan precedents.
  • Land grants to Brahmins and temples increase; feudal tendencies emerge with samantas.
  • Varna hierarchy reinforced; Smritis (Manusmriti revisions), Dharmashastras codify social norms.

Economy & Trade

  • Silk Road and maritime trade flourish; towns connect to Persia, Arabia, Byzantium, Sri Lanka, China (Fahien notes perilous sea route).
  • Gold (Dinar), silver, copper coinage abundant; metallurgical excellence (iron pillar of Mehrauli).
  • Agriculture supported by irrigation tanks, canals; land measurement systems recorded in inscriptions.

Religion & Thought

  • Hinduism (Vaishnavism, Saivism) ascendant; Gupta kings adopt Vaishnava titles, yet patronise Buddhism (Nalanda) and Jainism.
  • Bhakti devotion grows alongside classical sacrifices; temples with shikhara and garbha-griha standardised (Dashavatara temple, Deogarh).
  • Buddhism remains influential; Fahien documents monastic life, Vinaya adherence, and pilgrimage circuits.

Arts & Sciences

  • Literature: Kalidasa (Abhijnana Shakuntalam), Vishakhadatta (Mudrarakshasa), Bhasa plays revived.
  • Sciences: Aryabhata (Aryabhatiya), Varahamihira (Brihatsamhita), Sushruta Samhita revisions.
  • Visual Arts: Ajanta paintings (Gupta phase), Sarnath Buddha images (smooth finish), Mathura school red sandstone icons.

Trans-Regional Contacts

  • Fahien from China studies monastic discipline, notes absence of harsh criminal law, recorded into travelogue.
  • Trade with China yields silk, while India exports gems, spices, textiles; diplomatic exchanges with Sassanian Persia.
  • Scholars from Tibet, China, Korea visit Nalanda (text mentions students from China, Japan, Mongolia, Sri Lanka).

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Remember administrative hierarchy and titles (uparika, vishyapati) for governance questions.
  • Use Fahien's observations for social history, especially criminal justice and monastic discipline.
  • Link Gupta art developments to temple architecture timeline.
Critic's Calibration: Avoid romantic "Golden Age" cliché—back arguments with evidence on land grants, Hun pressure, and regional variations. Explicitly cite Chinese pilgrim Fahien to show cross-continental testimony.
  • Balanced achievements with structural stresses (feudalisation, Hun invasions).
  • Included trans-Asian scholarly flow (Nalanda attracting China, Korea, Tibet).
  • Mapped coinage and trade to demonstrate economic vibrancy.

Lesson 8 · Harsha and the Regional Kingdoms

Timeframe · c. 600 – 750 CE Post-Gupta fragmentation leads to Harsha's brief North Indian union alongside powerful regional dynasties (Chalukyas, Pallavas, Eastern Chalukyas) shaping politics, religion and India–China diplomacy.

Chronological Spine

  1. 606 CE · Harsha's Accession
    In 606 CE, Harsha of the Pushyabhuti line ascended at Thanesar after his brother’s murder, uniting Kannauj and northern India through rapid campaigns, which explains when a post-Gupta integrator emerged.
  2. 606 – 612 CE · Consolidation
    Between 606 and 612 CE, Harsha subdued Sind, Bengal and Nepal, appointed relatives as viceroys and balanced Shaiva, Buddhist and Jain patronage, illustrating how he stabilised the Ganga plain.
  3. c. 612 – 630 CE · Expansion & Administration
    From about 612 to 630 CE, Harsha moved his capital to Kannauj, held quinquennial alms assemblies at Prayaga and governed through officials like Bhandi, showing how ritual generosity underpinned political legitimacy.
  4. 630 – 643 CE · Hieun Tsang's Visit
    Between 630 and 643 CE, the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang) traversed Harsha’s domains, documenting monasteries, cities and just governance, which later informed Tang court diplomacy with India.
  5. c. 630 CE · Encounter with Chalukyas
    Around 630 CE, Harsha’s southern thrust was checked by Chalukya king Pulakeshin II on the Narmada, delineating a pragmatic north–south frontier between Kannauj and Vatapi.
  6. 647 CE · Harsha's Death & Fragmentation
    When Harsha died childless in 647 CE, officials briefly detained a usurper with Chinese help, but the empire soon splintered into regional states, paving the way for the later tripartite struggle over Kannauj.

Administration & Society

  • Harsha's administration retains Gupta-style provinces; officials include mahasandhivigrahika (foreign minister), mahapratihara (frontier defence).
  • Land revenue core income; villages function as fiscal units with collective responsibility.
  • Hieun Tsang notes charitable hospitals, free rest houses, limited criminal punishments.

Religion & Culture

  • Harsha initially Shaiva, later Mahayana Buddhist patron; convenes assemblies at Kannauj/Prayaga inviting monks, distributes wealth.
  • Authored plays (Ratnavali, Priyadarsika) reflecting court culture; Banabhatta's Harshacharita chronicles life.
  • Monasteries (Nalanda) reach apogee; Hieun Tsang praises its curriculum and global student body (China, Japan, Mongolia, Sri Lanka, Tibet).

Regional Powers

  • Chalukyas of Badami (Pulakesin II) control Deccan, repel Harsha; maritime trade via western seaboard.
  • Pallavas (Simhavishnu, Mahendravarman I, Narasimhavarman I) dominate south-eastern coast, clash with Chalukyas.
  • Eastern Chalukyas, Pandyas, Kamarupa maintain autonomy; Kashmir (Karkota) emerging.

India–China Interface

  • T'ang emperor Tai Tsung sends embassy (c. 640 CE); Harsha reciprocates, later Chinese envoy intervenes post-Harsha succession.
  • Hieun Tsang's records compare India's urban density to China, note Kanauj's elegance.
  • Exchange of relics/texts: Hieun Tsang carries Buddhist scriptures relics back to China.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Use Harsha's quinquennial assemblies to discuss royal charity & religious policy.
  • Highlight Pulakesin II vs Harsha (Aihole inscription) for interstate relations.
  • Cite Hieun Tsang for socio-economic descriptions (Pataliputra's decline, Kanauj's rise).
Critic's Calibration: Underline that Harsha's empire was a loose confederacy; avoid projecting long-lasting unity. Stress diplomatic exchanges with China (embassy, prisoner transfer) as evidence of international stature.
  • Incorporated regional powers to avoid Delhi-centric narrative.
  • Documented Hieun Tsang's stay (630–643 CE) and testimonies.
  • Tagged literary outputs (Banabhatta, Harsha) for culture questions.

Lesson 9 · Cultural Developments in South India (4th – 9th centuries)

Timeframe · c. 300 – 900 CE Pallavas, Chalukyas and Pandya resurgence reshape religious expressions, temple architecture, Bhakti movements, and maritime trade linking Coromandel to China and Southeast Asia.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 4th – 6th centuries CE · Early Pallava/Chalukya Foundations
    During the 4th–6th centuries CE, Pallavas established control over Tondaimandalam from Kanchipuram while the Early Chalukyas fortified Badami, marking where peninsular heartlands reorganised after Satavahana decline.
  2. c. 575 – 630 CE · Simhavishnu & Mahendravarman I
    Between 575 and 630 CE, Simhavishnu and Mahendravarman I repelled Kalabhra usurpers, authored Tamil-Sanskrit works like Mattavilasa Prahasana and experimented with rock-cut shrines at Mandagappattu, showing how cultural synthesis legitimised rule.
  3. 630 – 668 CE · Narasimhavarman I (Mamalla)
    From 630 to 668 CE, Narasimhavarman I defeated Pulakeshin II, captured Vatapi, and commissioned the shore temples and monolithic rathas at Mamallapuram, explaining when the Pallavas projected maritime prowess.
  4. 7th – 8th centuries CE · Bhakti Surge
    Throughout the 7th–8th centuries CE, Tamil Shaiva Nayanmars and Vaishnava Alvars sang Tevaram and Divya Prabandham hymns in temple towns like Chidambaram and Srirangam, demonstrating how emotive bhakti reshaped worship.
  5. c. 700 – 750 CE · Rajasimha & Structural Temples
    Around 700–750 CE, Pallava king Rajasimha built structural stone temples such as Kanchipuram’s Kailasanatha while the Chalukyas at Pattadakal blended Nagara and Dravida styles, revealing how architects moved beyond rock-cut prototypes.
  6. 8th – 9th centuries CE · Maritime Commerce
    In the 8th–9th centuries CE, ports like Nagapattinam and Mamallapuram sent textiles and spices to Sri Lanka, Srivijaya and China under guilds like the Ayyavole 500, highlighting why peninsular polities courted overseas trade.

Art & Architecture

  • Pallava innovations: rock-cut (Mahendravarman) to structural temples (Narasimhavarman II), Pancha Rathas, Shore Temple, Kailasanatha.
  • Chalukyan art: Badami cave temples, Aihole experimentation, Pattadakal UNESCO ensemble blending North-South idioms.
  • Buddhist and Jain cave patronage continues (Ellora multi-faith complex).

Bhakti & Literature

  • Nayanmars (Appar, Sambandar, Sundarar) and Alvars (Nammalvar, Andal) compose hymns emphasising personal devotion.
  • Bhakti hagiographies (Periyapuranam later) memorialise saints, linking religion with social inclusivity.
  • Sanskrit prestige rises (Mahendravarman's Sanskrit play), alongside Tamil devotional canon codification.

Religion & Society

  • Temples evolve into socio-economic institutions controlling land, irrigation (tank maintenance) and artisan guild patronage.
  • Competition among Saivism, Vaishnavism, Jainism, Buddhism; royal patronage shifts (Mahendravarman's swing from Jainism to Saivism).
  • Hero-stone (viragal) tradition commemorates martial valour; temple dance, music integrated into ritual.

Trade & Overseas Links

  • Sea routes connect Coromandel to Sri Lanka, China, Burma, Southeast Asia; Mamallapuram thrives as seaport.
  • Export items include textiles, spices, pearls, medicinal plants; imports include silk, ceramics (Chinese rouletted ware evidence).
  • Merchant guilds (Manigramam, Ayyavole) establish overseas bases (Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia), linking temples to commerce.

Political Dynamics

  • Chalukya-Pallava wars define Deccan politics; Narasimhavarman I's victory at Vatapi (642 CE) celebrated in architecture.
  • Pandya revival (Nedunjeliyan III) expands to Kaveri region; fosters pearl trade.
  • Kalabhra interregnum marks social flux preceding Pallava resurgence.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate rock-cut vs structural temple phases with patron names.
  • Connect Bhakti movement to socio-political contexts (Kalabhra displacement, need for social cohesion).
  • Quote trade links to China and Southeast Asia when discussing early medieval economy.
Critic's Calibration: Ensure Bhakti is not seen as isolated piety—tie it to temple economy and maritime trade. Highlight Chinese trade references (Mamallapuram exports) to capture global dimension.

Lesson 10 · Arabs and Turks in the Indian Subcontinent

Timeframe · 7th – 16th centuries CE From maritime Arab networks to Turkic conquests and Delhi Sultanate governance, this phase integrates India into the wider Islamic world while confronting Mongol pressures and experimenting with administration.

Chronological Spine

  1. 7th century CE · Arab Maritime Contacts
    By the 7th century CE, Arab Muslim merchants frequented Kerala’s Muziris and Tamil ports, marrying into local communities and building mosques at Calicut, which explains how Islam first arrived peacefully by sea.
  2. 712 CE · Muhammad bin Qasim's Sind Campaign
    In 712 CE, Umayyad general Muhammad bin Qasim captured Debal, Sind and Multan to punish pirates and secure the Indus delta, creating the earliest Muslim administration in the subcontinent.
  3. 1000 – 1027 CE · Mahmud of Ghazni
    Between 1000 and 1027 CE, Mahmud of Ghazni led 17 raids from Afghanistan into Punjab and Gujarat, sacking Somnath to finance his capital’s art and science, and leaving behind Persian chroniclers like al-Biruni.
  4. 1175 – 1206 CE · Muhammad Ghori
    From 1175 to 1206 CE, Muhammad Ghori subdued Multan, Sind and the Rajput heartland after the second battle of Tarain, then left his slave-general Qutb-ud-din Aibak to establish the Delhi Sultanate, showing how conquest became governance.
  5. 1206 – 1526 CE · Delhi Sultanate Dynasties
    Between 1206 and 1526 CE, five Delhi Sultanate houses—Mamluk, Khalji, Tughlaq, Sayyid and Lodi—ruled from Delhi’s citadel, balancing iqta assignments, Persianate culture and Mongol threats to weld north India into a new polity.
  6. 1290s – 1350s CE · Reforms & Challenges
    During the 1290s–1350s, Alauddin Khalji imposed price controls and strict branding of horses to fund frontier armies, while Muhammad bin Tughlaq’s token currency and Daulatabad move backfired, demonstrating how experimentation accompanied expansion.
  7. 1398 CE · Timur's Invasion
    In 1398 CE, Timur sacked Delhi en route to Samarkand, massacring citizens and shattering the Tughlaq treasury, which accelerated provincial breakaways and set the stage for later Mughal claims.

Administration & Institutions

  • Iqta system: land revenue assignments to nobles (muqti) balancing military service; central audit by diwan-i-wizarat.
  • Siyasatnama traditions emphasise strong bureaucracy; Barid (intelligence), Ariz-i-mamalik (military affairs).
  • Shariat courts coexist with local customs; sultans negotiate with ulema and Sufi pirs.

Economy & Reforms

  • Alauddin Khalji fixes grain prices, controls markets (Diwan-i-Riyasat), enforces rationing for soldiers.
  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq's token currency (brass/copper) emulated Chinese experiments but failed due to forgery.
  • Large irrigation works under Firoz Tughlaq (canals from Yamuna to Hissar) boost agrarian output; kharaj fixed at one-third of produce.

Military & External Threats

  • Mongol (Chagatai) incursions frequent; Alauddin fortifies frontier (Siri fort), organises standing army with dagh (branding) & chehra (rolls).
  • Turkic cavalry tactics emphasise mobility; war-elephants supplement defense.
  • Timur's invasion demonstrates vulnerability to Central Asian powers.

Culture & Society

  • Delhi becomes cosmopolitan with Persianate culture; Sufi orders (Chishti, Suhrawardi) spread, fostering syncretic devotion.
  • Architecture: Qutub Minar, Alai Darwaza, Tughlaqabad illustrate evolving Indo-Islamic styles blending arches/domes with local ornamentation.
  • Literature: Amir Khusrau (Hindavi-Persian poetry), Ziauddin Barani (Tarikh-i-Firozshahi) provide court chronicles.

Regional Ripple Effects

  • Arab maritime presence influences Kerala's Mappila community, Gulf trade ties.
  • Turkish expansion triggers Rajput resistance (Mewar), emergence of Bahmani and Vijayanagara states in Deccan.
  • European travellers (Ibn Battuta) record Delhi's administration; Chinese knowledge of token currency acknowledged by Muhammad bin Tughlaq's experiment.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Chronicle Muhammad bin Tughlaq's policies (token currency, Doab taxation, capital shift) with outcomes.
  • Differentiate iqta system across dynasties (hereditary tendencies by Tughlaqs).
  • Quote Mongol threat frequency to contextualise military reforms.
Critic's Calibration: Resist simplistic "tyrant" portrayals—link reforms to geopolitical pressures. Explicitly mention Chinese precedent for token currency to underline comparative administrative consciousness.

Lesson 11 · Later Cholas and Pandyas

Timeframe · c. 850 – 1350 CE Imperial Cholas and resurgent Pandyas consolidate agrarian wealth, temple-centred administration, naval prowess and extensive Indian Ocean trade reaching China and beyond.

Chronological Spine

  1. 850 – 871 CE · Vijayalaya Chola
    Between 850 and 871 CE, Vijayalaya Chola seized Thanjavur from the Muttaraiyars, built a Durga temple and revived Chola authority in the Kaveri delta, signalling where a new agrarian core emerged.
  2. 907 – 955 CE · Parantaka I
    From 907 to 955 CE, Parantaka I pushed into Pandya territory, reorganised brahmadeya villages and invested in irrigation tanks, explaining how Chola administration monetised paddy belts.
  3. 985 – 1014 CE · Rajaraja I
    Between 985 and 1014 CE, Rajaraja I captured northern Sri Lanka and the Malabar coast, built the Brihadisvara Temple at Thanjavur with precise land surveys, and issued detailed inscriptions on temple endowments.
  4. 1012 – 1044 CE · Rajendra I
    During 1012–1044 CE, Rajendra I led naval expeditions to Srivijaya, marched to the Ganga, founded Gangaikonda Cholapuram and commemorated victories with massive water tanks, revealing the Cholas’ blue-water ambitions.
  5. 1070 – 1122 CE · Kulottunga I
    From 1070 to 1122 CE, Kulottunga I united the Chola and Eastern Chalukya lines, rationalised taxation and extended trade with Song China, keeping moneylenders and temple corporations at the heart of governance.
  6. 12th – 13th centuries CE · Later Chola Decline
    In the 12th–13th centuries CE, Hoysala and Pandya offensives eroded Chola frontiers even though temple economies thrived, indicating why military overreach and new rivals precipitated decline.
  7. c. 1216 – 1310 CE · Pandyan Resurgence
    Between 1216 and 1310 CE, Jatavarman Sundara Pandya conquered Sri Lanka and Kongu, endowed ports at Kayal that impressed traveller Marco Polo, and reasserted Tamil control over pearl and horse trade.
  8. 1310 – 1323 CE · Malik Kafur & Delhi Intervention
    From 1310 to 1323 CE, Alauddin Khalji’s general Malik Kafur raided Madurai, extracted tribute and intervened in Pandyan succession feuds, paving the way for the short-lived Madurai Sultanate.

Sources & Historiography

  • 10,000+ inscriptions (stone, copper plates) record land grants, temple endowments, social stratification.
  • Literature: Kalingattupparani, Muvarula, Kamba Ramayanam, Nannul, Mathavilasa Prahasanam (earlier Pallava) contextualise cultural milieu.
  • Foreign accounts: Chinese geographer (13th c.) noting 60,000 war elephants; Marco Polo's description of Kayal's multi-continental trade.

Administration & Local Bodies

  • Monarchy legitimised via divine titles (Peruman, Chakravarti); coronation suffix "deva".
  • Provinces (mandalam) like Cholamandalam, Tondaimandalam; viceroys (e.g., Chola-Pandya, Chola-Lankeswara) administer annexed zones.
  • Grassroots institutions: ur (peasant assemblies), sabha (brahmadeya councils), nagaram (mercantile guilds), nattar (regional federations) manage irrigation, taxation, justice.

Army & Navy

  • Standing army divisions: infantry, cavalry (kudirai sevagar), elephant corps (anaiyatkal); regimental ranks perundanam/cirudanam.
  • Navy controls Bay of Bengal; overseas expeditions to Sri Lanka, Maldives, Sri Vijaya, Kadaram (Kedah).
  • Military holdings (padaipparru) granted to soldiers; cantonments (padaividu) secure capitals.

Economy & Trade

  • Intensive wet-rice agriculture enabled by river-fed irrigation tanks, sluices, anicuts; temples manage water distribution.
  • Temples act as corporate landlords, credit agencies, craft patrons; endowments include land, gold, livestock, lamps.
  • Maritime trade: ports like Nagapattinam, Kaveripattinam, Kayal receive ships from Arabia, China, Southeast Asia; exports include rice, textiles, horses, pearls.

Religion & Culture

  • Saivism and Vaishnavism flourish; temple rituals codified (Agamas) with festivals, temple kitchens feeding pilgrims.
  • Bhakti literature codified (Saiva canon Tevaram, Vaishnava Nalayira Divya Prabandham); Shaiva Siddhanta philosophy crystallises.
  • Art & architecture: Brihadisvara (Thanjavur), Gangaikonda Cholapuram, Airavatesvara (Darasuram) display Dravidian vimana, sculpted mandapas; bronze casting (Nataraja) reaches finesse.

Pandya Specifics

  • Capital Madurai controls Vaigai irrigation; Tirunelveli exports grain, cotton, bullocks to Malabar and Southeast Asia.
  • Temples like Meenakshi-Sundareswarar become socio-economic centres; donors include merchants, artisans, guilds.
  • Delhi Sultanate interventions (Malik Kafur) trigger decline; Vijayanagara later incorporates Pandya territories.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Quote local self-government (Chola sabha committees: eri-variyam for tanks, pon-variyam for gold) to illustrate decentralisation.
  • Highlight overseas trade missions to China (Song dynasty records) when writing on Indian Ocean commerce.
  • Use Marco Polo and Chinese geographer accounts for external corroboration.
Critic's Calibration: Present Chola state as networked through assemblies rather than absolute monarchy. Stress China-Arabia maritime linkages via Kayal and Nagapattinam for global trade context.
  • Combined inscriptional, literary, and foreign traveller evidence.
  • Brought out role of local bodies and temple economy in governance.
  • Integrated Pandya resurgence and Delhi Sultanate encounters for continuity.

Lesson 12 · South Indian Kingdoms II: Chalukyas & Rashtrakutas

Timeframe · 6th – 10th centuries CE Badami and Vengi Chalukyas alongside Rashtrakutas directed Deccan politics, commemorated through Aihole epigraphy, Pattadakal monuments and copper-plate charters.

Chronological Spine

  1. c. 543 CE · Pulakeshin I
    Around 543 CE, Pulakeshin I fortified Vatapi (Badami) and assumed the title Satyashraya, laying the foundation for Western Chalukya control over the Malaprabha basin.
  2. 609 – 642 CE · Pulakeshin II
    Between 609 and 642 CE, Pulakeshin II defeated Harsha on the Narmada (as recorded in the Aihole inscription), checked the Pallavas and granted land to Brahmanas, exemplifying Deccan statecraft.
  3. 642 – 753 CE · Eastern Chalukyas
    From 642 to 753 CE, Eastern Chalukya rulers governed from Vengi, mediating Pallava–Chola rivalries and spreading Telugu inscriptions that influenced later coastal culture.
  4. 753 – 814 CE · Dantidurga & Govinda III
    During 753–814 CE, Dantidurga toppled the Chalukyas and, along with Govinda III, led Rashtrakuta cavalry to Kanchipuram and Kannauj, showing how the Deccan briefly dominated pan-Indian politics.
  5. 814 – 880 CE · Amoghavarsha I
    Between 814 and 880 CE, Amoghavarsha I ruled from Manyakheta, patronised Jain scholars and composed Kavirajamarga, highlighting when Kannada literature and Jain patronage flourished.
  6. 973 CE onwards · Tailapa II
    After 973 CE, Tailapa II revived Chalukya authority from Kalyani, displacing the waning Rashtrakutas and initiating the Later Chalukya architectural florescence seen at Lakkundi and Gadag.

Sources

  • Aihole, Badami, Pattadakal inscriptions chart conquests.
  • Navsari, Sanjan plates note Rashtrakuta grants to Brahmins and Jain institutions.
  • Arab geographers mention Deccan cotton ports and horse trade.

Polity & Administration

  • Mandala structure with rashtras, visayas, and villages under hereditary chiefs.
  • Feudatories (mahamandalesvaras) rewarded with titles, expected to provide troops.
  • Eastern Chalukya marriages with Cholas lay groundwork for later dynastic fusion.

Economy

  • Black-soil agriculture (jowar, cotton) supplemented by tank irrigation.
  • Coastal ports—Chaul, Goa, Sopara—handle Arab-Persian shipping.
  • Guilds such as Ayyavole 500 and Manigramam receive tax remissions for temple upkeep.

Culture & Faith

  • Pattadakal temples blend Nagara and Dravida idioms; Badami caves showcase Vaishnava, Shaiva, Jain iconography.
  • Kavirajamarga, Vikramarjuna Vijaya highlight Kannada literary bloom.
  • Jainism flourishes under Amoghavarsha; Shaivism/Vaishnavism co-exist with Buddhist residues.

Trans-Regional Links

  • Embassies to Abbasid Caliphate recorded in Arab texts.
  • Rashtrakuta raids on Kannauj alter north Indian balance during Gurjara-Pratihara era.
  • Eastern Chalukya ports funnel Chinese ceramics and Southeast Asian spices inland.

Lesson 13 · Imperial Cholas: Oceanic Networks

Timeframe · 9th – 13th centuries CE Vijayalaya through Kulottunga fused sabha-based governance, naval muscle and temple economies, documenting every fiscal detail in inscriptions.

Chronological Spine

  1. 850 CE · Vijayalaya
    In 850 CE, Vijayalaya’s capture of Thanjavur symbolised the rebirth of Chola naval and agrarian power that later controlled Bay of Bengal trade routes.
  2. 907 – 955 CE · Parantaka I
    Parantaka I’s 907–955 CE reign consolidated the Kaveri delta through brahmadeya grants and temple-centered redistribution, demonstrating how ritual and revenue were intertwined.
  3. 985 – 1014 CE · Rajaraja I
    Rajaraja I (985–1014 CE) extended fleets to the Maldives, reformed revenue surveys recorded on temple walls and set a template for Chola oceanic engagement.
  4. 1012 – 1044 CE · Rajendra I
    Rajendra I’s 1012–1044 CE conquests of Srivijaya and the Gangetic plains celebrated by the Cholaganga tank show how overseas warfare reinforced imperial prestige.
  5. 1070 – 1120 CE · Kulottunga I
    Between 1070 and 1120 CE, Kulottunga I harmonised Eastern Chalukya and Chola interests, lightened land taxes and cultivated Chinese trade missions docking at Nagapattinam.
  6. 12th – 13th centuries · Decline
    In the 12th–13th centuries, repeated Hoysala, Pandya and Delhi incursions fractured Chola rule even as autonomous merchant and temple corporations continued to manage irrigation and commerce.

Governance

  • Mandalam → valanadu → nadu → kurram layering with village juries (kudavolai ballots).
  • Sabha committees (eri-variyam, totta-variyam, pon-variyam) manage tanks, gardens, treasury.
  • Royal viceroys (Chola-Pandya, Chola-Lankeswara) oversee conquered provinces.

Military & Navy

  • Standing regiments (Velaikkaras) named after monarchs; elephant and cavalry corps documented.
  • Blue-water navy dominates Bay of Bengal, enabling overseas raids and escorting merchant convoys.
  • Fortified capitals (Thanjavur, Gangaikonda Cholapuram) double as logistic hubs.

Economy

  • Deltaic irrigation (Grand Anicut expansions) underpins paddy surpluses.
  • Temples act as banks, landlords, workshop patrons employing metal casters, textile artisans.
  • Ports—Nagapattinam, Kaveripattinam, Kayal—exchange rice, textiles, beads for horses, aromatics, Chinese ceramics.

Culture

  • Saiva/ Vaishnava Bhakti codified; Periyapuranam, Kamba Ramayanam flourish.
  • Bronze casting (Nataraja) epitomises lost-wax mastery; murals adorn temple corridors.
  • Buddhist grants (Nagapattinam vihara) reveal cosmopolitan patronage.

Sources

  • Uttiramerur inscriptions describe sabha elections; Tiruvalangadu plates narrate conquests.
  • Foreign travellers (Zhao Rugua, Marco Polo) corroborate trade/elephant data.
  • Temple copper plates meticulously register land surveys, taxes, endowments.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Contrast Brihadisvara vs Gangaikonda Cholapuram for architectural evolution.
  • Mention Chola–Srivijaya interactions when discussing Indian Ocean trade.
Critic's Calibration: Counter “centralised absolutism” claims by emphasising recorded sabha autonomy and audit mechanisms.
  • Integrated military, irrigation, and trade data.
  • Highlighted inscriptional evidence for governance.

Lesson 14 · Spread of Indian Culture in Asia

Timeframe · 3rd century BCE – 15th century CE Ashokan missions, Buddhist monks, Tamil guilds and Hindu monarchs diffused Indic scripts, religion, art and polity to Sri Lanka, Central Asia, China, Indo-China and the Malay Archipelago.

Chronological Spine

  1. 3rd century BCE · Ashokan outreach
    In the 3rd century BCE, Ashoka sent his son Mahinda and daughter Sangamitta to Sri Lanka with the Bodhi sapling and inscribed edicts referencing Hellenistic kings, showing how Mauryan diplomacy globalised Dhamma.
  2. 1st – 5th centuries CE · Central Asian Silk Route
    From the 1st to 5th centuries CE, monks and merchants carried Sanskrit manuscripts via Khotan and Kashgar, enabling translators like Kumarajiva to render Buddhist texts into Chinese at Chang’an.
  3. 4th – 9th centuries CE · Mainland Southeast Asia
    Between the 4th and 9th centuries CE, Funan, Chenla and Champa courts adopted Sanskrit titles, Indian law codes and brick temple forms, revealing why Indic ideas reshaped Mekong-Red River polities.
  4. 7th – 11th centuries CE · Sri Vijaya & Sailendra
    During the 7th–11th centuries CE, the Srivijaya and Sailendra dynasties of Sumatra and Java invited monks from Nalanda, carved Borobudur stupas and exchanged embassies with the Pallavas and Cholas to control the Straits of Malacca.
  5. 12th – 13th centuries CE · Angkor apogee
    In the 12th–13th centuries CE, Khmer king Suryavarman II built Angkor Wat with concentric moats mirroring South Asian temple-cosmology, highlighting where hydraulic engineering and Vaishnava imagery merged.
  6. 13th – 15th centuries CE · Localisation
    From the 13th to 15th centuries CE, Theravada Buddhism spread through Burma and Thailand, Balinese Hinduism preserved ancient rituals, and Indian epics inspired Southeast Asian dance-drama, showing how imported motifs were localised.

Transmission Channels

  • Monks (Fahien, Xuanzang, I-Tsing) traverse Silk Roads; Tamil guild merchants sail from Nagapattinam.
  • Sanskrit/Pali as prestige languages; Tamil/Prakrit inscriptions discovered in Thailand, Indonesia.
  • Diplomatic exchanges (Chola–Sailendra letters, Khmer envoys) sustain networks.

Sri Lanka & Central Asia

  • Mahavamsa chronicles Buddhist lineages; Sigiriya frescoes show artistic borrowings.
  • Khotan manuscripts, Bamiyan Buddhas (pre-destruction) reflect Gandharan hybrid styles.
  • Tibetan kings (Songtsen Gampo) invite Indian scholars, establishing Lhasa as Buddhist hub.

China & Tibet

  • Xuanzang studies at Nalanda; returns with texts influencing Chinese Buddhism.
  • Translation bureaus in Chang’an integrate Indian logic, medicine.
  • Tibetan Potala-era art retains Pala-Sena stylistic cues.

Indo-China & Khmer Realm

  • Champa dedicates temples to Shiva-Bhadresvara; inscriptions mention Kaundinya legend.
  • Angkor Wat bas-reliefs narrate Ramayana/Mahabharata episodes.
  • Pagan (Myanmar) stupas integrate Sinhalese and Pala features.

Malay Archipelago

  • Sri Vijaya dominates Malacca Strait; Sailendra builds Borobudur with Indian mandala layout.
  • Bali preserves caste-like social structure and Hindu rituals into modern era.
  • Javanese wayang kulit retells Ramayana/Mahabharata with local aesthetics.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Pair site-patron combos (Borobudur–Sailendra, Angkor Wat–Suryavarman II).
  • Cite Nagapattinam vihara grant to evidence South India–Sri Vijaya ties.
  • Use Chinese pilgrim accounts for cross-regional cultural proof.
Critic's Calibration: Frame diffusion as dialogic—local elites selectively adapted Indic elements instead of passive reception.
  • Mapped both overland and maritime conduits.
  • Distinguished regional trajectories (Sri Lanka vs Khmer vs Java).
  • Linked epigraphic/art evidence to textual references.

Lesson 15 · Early Medieval India: Rajputs & External Raids

Timeframe · 7th – 12th centuries CE Rajput polities, Arab conquest of Sind, Mahmud of Ghazni’s raids and Muhammad Ghori’s victories reconfigured north Indian power.

Chronological Spine

  1. 7th – 8th centuries · Rajput ascendancy
    In the 7th–8th centuries CE, Rajput clans like the Gurjara-Pratiharas, Chauhans and Paramaras fortified Rajasthan and Malwa, controlling caravan routes and temples to assert kshatriya status.
  2. 712 CE · Muhammad bin Qasim
    In 712 CE, Muhammad bin Qasim led Umayyad armies to annex Sind and Multan, granting dhimmi protections to Buddhists and Hindus while securing Arab access to Indian Ocean trade.
  3. 1001 – 1027 CE · Mahmud of Ghazni
    Between 1001 and 1027 CE, Mahmud of Ghazni repeatedly raided the Gangetic frontier, capturing Mathura and Somnath to finance his capital and to claim the title ‘Defender of the Faith’.
  4. 1175 – 1186 CE · Ghori’s Punjab campaigns
    From 1175 to 1186 CE, Muhammad Ghori dismantled Ghaznavid remnants in Lahore and seized Multan and Sind, establishing forward bases that threatened Rajput domains.
  5. 1191 – 1192 CE · Battles of Tarain
    In 1191–1192 CE near present-day Haryana, Prithviraj Chauhan first repelled then lost to Muhammad Ghori at Tarain; the defeat opened Delhi’s gates to Turkic commanders.
  6. 1194 CE · Battle of Chandawar
    At Chandawar in 1194 CE, Ghori defeated Gahadavala king Jayachandra near the Yamuna, annexing Kannauj and clearing the path for sustained Sultanate expansion.

Rajput Polity

  • Clan-based martial ethos, intermarriage, and fort-centric defence.
  • Tripartite struggle (Pratihara–Pala–Rashtrakuta) drains strength.
  • Limited confederacy; rivalry prevents united front against invaders.

Arab Sind

  • Qasim administers Sind through local elites; Hindu/Buddhist communities classed as zimmis.
  • Indian numerals, astronomy, medicine transmit westward via Sind.
  • Ports like Debal and Mansura thrive under Arab oversight.

Mahmud of Ghazni

  • Targets wealthy temples/cities; Waihind, Thaneshwar, Mathura, Somnath notable.
  • Patronises Firdausi (Shahnama) and Alberuni (Kitab-i-Hind).
  • Annexes Punjab, reshaping frontier security.

Muhammad Ghori

  • Seeks territorial control, not just plunder.
  • Tarain/Chandawar victories dismantle Chauhan and Gahadavala power.
  • Leaves Qutb-ud-din Aibak to consolidate, paving way for Sultanate.

Causes of Rajput Failure

  • Factionalism, lack of centralised coordination.
  • Reliance on elephants vs Turkic cavalry archers and mobility.
  • Kshatriya-only military recruitment limited manpower compared to Turkic meritocracy.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Distinguish Mahmud’s raids from Ghori’s conquests.
  • Memorise Tarain (1191/1192) and Chandawar (1194) chronology.
  • Cite Alberuni for contemporary observations.
Critic's Calibration: Analyse structural weaknesses (political, military) rather than cultural determinism when explaining defeats.
  • Connected Sind episode to knowledge transfer narratives.
  • Differentiated raid vs conquest motives.
  • Noted Rajput inter-dynastic rivalries as key variable.

Lesson 16 · Delhi Sultanate: Dynasties & Reforms

Timeframe · 1206 – 1526 CE Mamluk, Khalji, Tughlaq, Sayyid and Lodi rulers forged iqta administration, resisted Mongols, and experimented with markets, currency and irrigation.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1206 – 1236 · Mamluks
    Between 1206 and 1236 CE, Qutb-ud-din Aibak and Iltutmish built Delhi’s Qutb complex, formalised iqta assignments and gained Caliphal investiture, laying institutional foundations for sultanate rule.
  2. 1290 – 1320 · Khaljis
    From 1290 to 1320 CE, Jalal-ud-din and Alauddin Khalji centralised power in Delhi, repelled Mongols, conquered Gujarat and the Deccan, and regulated grain markets to fund standing armies.
  3. 1320 – 1414 · Tughlaqs
    During 1320–1414 CE, Tughlaq rulers moved capitals, experimented with token currency and built canals yet faced rebellions, demonstrating how ambitious reforms strained legitimacy.
  4. 1414 – 1451 · Sayyids
    Between 1414 and 1451 CE, the Sayyid dynasty governed a shrunken Delhi amid Timurid aftershocks, relying on Afghan nobles even as provincial sultanates grew autonomous.
  5. 1451 – 1526 · Lodis
    From 1451 to 1526 CE, Afghan Lodis such as Sikandar expanded into Bihar and reorganised revenue but factional feuds weakened them, enabling Babur’s artillery-led victory at Panipat.
  6. 1290s – 1520s · Mongol & Timurid pressures
    Throughout 1290–1520 CE, repeated Mongol and Timurid incursions forced Delhi to fortify cities, maintain frontier garrisons and adapt cavalry tactics, influencing fiscal and military policy across dynasties.

Political Institutions

  • Chahalgani (group of forty) influences succession until Balban curbs them.
  • Iqtas granted for military service, audited by diwan-i-wizarat.
  • Provincial governors (muqtis) evolve into semi-hereditary nobility by late Tughlaq era.

Military & Frontier

  • Balban’s “blood and iron” frontier policy; Alauddin brands horses (dagh) and keeps rolls (chehra).
  • Mongol threats necessitate Siri fort, standing army stipends.
  • Deccan expeditions (Malik Kafur) extend influence but strain resources.

Economic Reforms

  • Alauddin’s market control (grain, cloth, horses) stabilises prices for army provisioning.
  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq’s token currency fails due to forgery; Doab taxation sparks revolts.
  • Firoz Tughlaq’s canals, gardens, hospitals display welfare orientation.

Religion & Culture

  • Persianate court culture; chroniclers (Barani, Afif) document politics.
  • Sufi khanqahs (Chishti) provide social support and mediate conflicts.
  • Architecture evolves from Qutub complex to Tughlaqabad, blending arches, domes, battlements.

Regional Ripple Effects

  • Bahmani and Vijayanagar states emerge partly in response to Delhi pressure.
  • Rajput, Bengal, Kashmir courts adopt selected Sultanate institutions.
  • Timur’s 1398 sack of Delhi accelerates provincial autonomy.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Match dynasty to flagship policy (e.g., Alauddin markets; MBT token currency).
  • Use iqta terminology accurately (muqti, watan jagir, khalisa).
  • Reference Mongol threat frequency when explaining reforms.
Critic's Calibration: Situate “failed” experiments (token currency, capital shift) within geopolitical compulsions rather than personal eccentricity.
  • Sequenced dynasties with policy highlights.
  • Connected military needs to fiscal reforms.
  • Noted regional reactions to Delhi’s expansion.

Lesson 17 · India under the Delhi Sultanate

Focus · Administration, Society, Economy, Culture Zooms into Sultanate governance mechanics, social life, economic activity, art-architecture, and literary output beyond dynastic chronology.

Chronological Spine

  1. 13th century · Administrative codification
    In the 13th century, Iltutmish standardised the iqta auditing system, dividing realms into shiqs, parganas and villages, which clarified how land revenue underpinned the Sultanate bureaucracy.
  2. 1290s – 1310s · Market & army reforms
    During the 1290s–1310s, Alauddin Khalji enforced price ceilings, rationed grain depots and branded horses to ensure his Delhi garrisons could repel Mongols without bankrupting peasants.
  3. 1320s – 1350s · Experimental governance
    Between the 1320s and 1350s, Muhammad bin Tughlaq introduced copper token currency, shifted nobles to Daulatabad and raised Doab taxes, bold experiments that triggered revolts and depopulation.
  4. 1351 – 1388 · Welfare turn
    From 1351 to 1388 CE, Firoz Tughlaq refocused on welfare—digging canals, founding hospitals, encouraging public works and even managing charitable slaves, showing a softer governance model.
  5. 15th century · Provincialisation
    In the 15th century, breakaway states like Bahmani, Bengal and Vijayanagar adapted Sultanate institutions—forts, revenue registers, Persian chancery styles—demonstrating administrative diffusion beyond Delhi.

Administrative Apparatus

  • Sultan advised by diwan-i-wizarat (finance), diwan-i-arz (military), diwan-i-insha (chancery).
  • Provincial units: iqta → shiq → pargana → village; local chiefs/khuts involved in revenue collection.
  • No fixed succession law; nobles/ulema influence enthronement.

Society

  • Urban cosmopolitanism—Persian, Turkic, Afghan elites alongside Indian converts, merchants, artisans.
  • Rural society pays kharaj (~1/3rd produce); village panchayats preserve customary practices.
  • Women of nobility wield influence (Razia, Shah Turkan); Sufi women participate in shrines.

Economy

  • Agriculture diversified (wheat, rice, cotton, sugarcane); new crops introduced via Persian contacts.
  • Karkhanas produce textiles, armaments, paper; bazaars regulated in Delhi/Lahore.
  • Trade networks connect Central Asia, Persian Gulf, Southeast Asia; customs duties significant revenue.

Art & Architecture

  • Qutub Minar, Alai Darwaza show arcuate construction using spolia.
  • Tughlaq architecture emphasises sloping walls, minimal ornamentation.
  • Provincial styles (Gujarat jharokhas, Bengal terracotta) blend local motifs.

Literature & Thought

  • Persian chronicles (Barani, Isami) document ideology.
  • Amir Khusrau pioneers Hindavi-Persian poetry, qawwali traditions.
  • Sanskrit works translated (Ayurveda, Bhagavata) under Firoz Tughlaq.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • List key diwans and their functions for admin answers.
  • Use Alauddin’s price schedule to discuss urban economy.
  • Quote traveller Ibn Battuta on Delhi markets and justice.
Critic's Calibration: Balance elite chronicles with material evidence (architecture, inscriptions) to avoid Delhi-centric biases.
  • Summarised governance layers from centre to village.
  • Integrated socio-economic and cultural data.
  • Highlighted traveller accounts as corroboration.

Lesson 18 · Bhakti Movement & Sufism

Timeframe · 8th – 17th centuries CE Saint-poets, reformers and Sufi pirs championed devotion, egalitarian ethics, vernacular expression and interfaith dialogues.

Chronological Spine

  1. 8th century · Sankara
    In the 8th century, Adi Shankara from Kaladi travelled across India establishing four mathas at Sringeri, Dwaraka, Puri and Badrinath to preach Advaita, demonstrating how scholastic pilgrimages revived Vedic monism.
  2. 11th – 12th centuries · Ramanuja & Basavanna
    During the 11th–12th centuries, Ramanuja of Srirangam articulated Vishishtadvaita and organised Sri Vaishnava temple services, while Basavanna in Kalyana led Lingayat sharanas who rejected caste and idol rituals.
  3. 12th – 13th centuries · Sufi khanqahs
    In the 12th–13th centuries, Sufi saints like Muinuddin Chishti at Ajmer and Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi set up khanqahs offering langar, qawwali and counselling, which blended Persian mysticism with Indian devotional vernaculars.
  4. 14th – 15th centuries · Nirguna saints
    Across the 14th–15th centuries, Ramananda, Kabir and Guru Nanak roamed the Ganga plains critiquing caste, idol worship and empty ritual, crafting vernacular verses that emphasised an omnipresent, formless divinity.
  5. 15th – 16th centuries · Vaishnava efflorescence
    Between the 15th and 16th centuries, Chaitanya in Bengal, Vallabhacharya in Mathura and Mirabai in Mewar mobilised bhakti through music, dance and pilgrimages, creating emotional communities around Krishna devotion.
  6. 17th century · Sikh institutionalisation
    In the 17th century, Guru Arjan compiled the Adi Granth and Guru Gobind Singh later created the Khalsa at Anandpur (1699), militarising Sikh identity in response to Mughal persecution.

Core Themes

  • Devotion (bhakti) accessible to all; Sufi zikr and sama stress love of God.
  • Saguna vs nirguna strands; Sufis emphasise fana (ego dissolution).
  • Vernacular poetry democratizes theology.

Regional Streams

  • South: Alvars, Nayanmars, Haridasas; Lingayat vachanas reject caste.
  • West: Warkari saints (Namdev, Tukaram), Sant tradition (Eknath, Dadu).
  • East/North: Chaitanya’s kirtans, Baul mystics, Nanak’s Sikh Panth.

Sufi Orders

  • Chishti (Ajmer, Delhi), Suhrawardi (Multan), Qadiri, Naqshbandi (court-aligned).
  • Dargahs offer langar, shelter, inter-community dialogue.
  • Malfuzat (discourses) and khanqah records preserve teachings.

Social Impact

  • Questions caste/gender hierarchies; women saints (Andal, Mirabai, Lal Ded) gain voice.
  • Artisans, peasants find solace in egalitarian bhajans/abhangs.
  • Bridges Hindu-Muslim cultural exchanges during Sultanate/Mughal periods.

Texts & Music

  • Tevaram, Divya Prabandham, Guru Granth Sahib, Vachanas, Abhangs.
  • Qawwalis at Sufi shrines, kirtans in Vaishnava temples, bhajans in satsangs.
  • Hagiographies (Periyapuranam, Bhakta Vijaya) record saintly lives.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate saguna/nirguna with saint examples.
  • Mention Sufi orders’ regional HQs for map questions.
  • Highlight socio-political contexts (Islamic influence, urbanisation) for Bhakti rise.
Critic's Calibration: Avoid overstating egalitarian outcomes—social hierarchies persisted despite reformist rhetoric.
  • Sequenced saints chronologically.
  • Connected doctrinal themes to social change.
  • Integrated Sufi-Bhakti dialogues without conflation.

Lesson 19 · Vijayanagar & Bahmani Kingdoms

Timeframe · 1336 – 1565 CE Twin Deccan powers managed irrigation, horse trade, temple construction and Indo-Persian court culture while contesting Raichur Doab.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1336 CE · Vijayanagar founded
    In 1336 CE, brothers Harihara and Bukka, guided by sage Vidyaranya, founded Vijayanagar on the Tungabhadra to resist Delhi incursions, creating a fortified capital at Hampi.
  2. 1347 CE · Bahmani sultanate
    By 1347 CE, Ala-ud-din Bahman Shah declared an independent Bahmani Sultanate at Gulbarga, organising the Deccan’s first Indo-Persian court separate from Delhi.
  3. 1422 – 1486 · Deva Raya II & Bahmani zenith
    Between 1422 and 1486 CE, Vijayanagar’s Deva Raya II imported horses through ports like Bhatkal while Bahmani rulers commanded Persianate administrators, showing how both states vied for the Krishna-Tungabhadra doab.
  4. 1469 – 1481 · Mahmud Gawan
    During 1469–1481 CE, Bahmani vizier Mahmud Gawan introduced centralised revenue surveys, built a renowned madrasa at Bidar and curbed amir factions, though his execution hastened the sultanate’s breakup.
  5. 1509 – 1529 · Krishnadevaraya
    From 1509 to 1529 CE, Vijayanagar emperor Krishnadevaraya captured Raichur, fostered Telugu literature via the Ashtadiggajas and financed monumental temples like Vittala, marking the empire’s golden age.
  6. 1565 · Talikota
    In 1565 CE at Talikota, a confederacy of Deccan sultanates defeated Regent Aliya Rama Raya, sacking Hampi and forcing the Vijayanagar court to relocate south of the Krishna river.

Sources

  • Srirangam, Hampi inscriptions; copper plates of Deva Raya.
  • Traveller accounts: Ibn Battuta, Nicolo Conti, Abdur Razzak, Domingo Paes, Nuniz.
  • Firishta’s chronicle for Bahmani history.

Administration

  • Vijayanagar’s Nayankara grants tie chiefs to state; capital organised into royal, temple, bazaar zones.
  • Bahmani taraf (province) under tarafdars; factionalism between Dakhanis and Afaqis.
  • Irrigation (Tungabhadra dam, canals) central to both economies.

Economy & Trade

  • Horse imports via Goa, Bhatkal; pepper, textiles exported to West Asia, Europe.
  • Bahmani access to diamond mines (Golconda) and Persian Gulf commerce.
  • Guilds (Komatis, Chettis) enjoy royal patronage, share temple endowments.

Culture

  • Vijayanagar temples (Virupaksha, Vittala) showcase Dravida architecture, musical pillars.
  • Bahmani art blends Persian tiles, stucco, arches (Bidar, Gulbarga).
  • Sufi shrines (Hazrat Gesu Daraz) and Virupaksha festivals illustrate pluralism.

Military & Diplomacy

  • Raichur Doab contested; artillery and foreign gunners introduced.
  • Portuguese ally with Vijayanagar for horses; Bahmani factions seek Ottoman/Persian aid.
  • Bahmani fragmentation into five Sultanates reshapes Deccan geopolitics.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Cite travellers for socio-economic descriptions.
  • Remember Talikota year (1565) and commanders.
  • Mention Mahmud Gawan’s reforms when discussing Bahmani governance.
Critic's Calibration: Talikota ends capital dominance but not Vijayanagar polity—note Aravidu continuation from Penukonda/Chandragiri.
  • Balanced Vijayanagar and Bahmani narratives.
  • Emphasised irrigation/horse trade as shared priorities.

Lesson 20 · The Mughal Empire: Political Narrative

Timeframe · 1526 – 1707 CE Babur’s artillery victory at Panipat set the stage for Humayun’s exile-return, Akbar’s integration, Jahangir-Shah Jahan’s cultural zenith, and Aurangzeb’s strained expansion.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1526 – 1530 · Babur
    Between 1526 and 1530 CE, Babur from Ferghana defeated Ibrahim Lodi at Panipat using matchlock infantry and field artillery, then chronicled his Indian campaigns in the Baburnama before dying at Agra.
  2. 1530 – 1556 · Humayun & Sur interlude
    From 1530 to 1556 CE, Humayun briefly ruled before losing to Sher Shah Suri, whose Sur Empire standardised the silver rupiya, postal roads and revenue surveys until Humayun—backed by Safavid Persia—recaptured Delhi.
  3. 1556 – 1605 · Akbar
    During 1556–1605 CE, Akbar consolidated the Mughal state from Agra, instituting mansabdari, abolishing jizya, hosting ibadat khana debates and conquering Gujarat, Bengal and parts of the Deccan under the principle of sulh-i-kul.
  4. 1605 – 1627 · Jahangir
    Between 1605 and 1627 CE, Jahangir balanced Nur Jahan’s powerful clique, dealt with Prince Khurram’s rebellion, patronised naturalist painting and welcomed English ambassador Thomas Roe to his court at Ajmer.
  5. 1628 – 1658 · Shah Jahan
    From 1628 to 1658 CE, Shah Jahan shifted the capital to Shahjahanabad (Delhi), commissioned the Taj Mahal and Red Fort, and pursued costly Central Asian and Deccan campaigns that strained finances.
  6. 1658 – 1707 · Aurangzeb
    During 1658–1707 CE, Aurangzeb imprisoned his father, expanded into the Deccan, reinstated jizya, and fought protracted wars against Marathas, Sikhs and Jats, ultimately stretching Mughal logistics to the breaking point.

Military & Expansion

  • Composite armies with artillery, cavalry, elephants under mansabdars.
  • Northwest stabilised via diplomacy; Deccan wars drain treasury.
  • Navy minimal—relied on alliances with coastal powers.

Administration

  • Mansabdari ranks (zat/sawar) link nobles to emperor; jagirs rotated.
  • Subahs governed by subahdars, diwans, qazis; faujdar ensures law/order.
  • Ain-i-Akbari documents revenue units (bigha, dam), crop schedules.

Religion & Policy

  • Akbar’s sulh-i-kul, abolition of jizya, Rajput alliances; Din-i-Ilahi limited circle.
  • Jahangir maintains tolerance; Aurangzeb’s orthodoxy reverses some concessions.
  • Sufi and Bhakti exchanges continue (Dara Shikoh’s Upanishad translations).

Culture

  • Mughal painting schools (Akbari realism, Jahangiri naturalism); architecture (Humayun’s Tomb, Fatehpur Sikri, Taj Mahal).
  • Literary minds: Abul Fazl, Faizi, Abdul Hamid Lahori.
  • Gardens (charbagh), calligraphy, pietra dura reflect Persian-Central Asian synthesis.

Decline Triggers

  • Jagir crisis, mansab inflation, court factionalism.
  • Regional uprisings (Marathas, Jats, Sikhs, Satnamis) strain resources.
  • European companies expand coastal footholds, altering trade revenues.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Sequence key battles (Panipat I/II, Khanwa, Haldighati, Deccan sieges).
  • Quote Ain-i-Akbari for administrative detail.
  • Contrast Sur reforms with Akbar’s systems to show continuity.
Critic's Calibration: Resist simplistic “decline after Aurangzeb” trope—link structural stresses (jagir crisis, regional dissent) with policy choices.
  • Covered full ruler sequence including Sur interregnum.
  • Integrated mansabdari-jagir mechanics.
  • Connected policy shifts to social coalitions.

Lesson 21 · India under the Mughals: Society & Economy

Focus · 16th – 18th centuries Mansabdari bureaucracy, agrarian relations, urban trade, cultural production and provincial politics under Mughal rule.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1560s – 1580s · Ain-i-Akbari
    In the 1560s–1580s, Abul Fazl compiled the Ain-i-Akbari at Akbar’s court in Fatehpur Sikri, documenting zabt revenue rates, provincial divisions and customs, providing a statistical blueprint for imperial governance.
  2. 1600s · Diversified nobility
    By the 1600s, Mughal mansabdari rosters in Agra and Lahore featured Rajput, Turani, Irani, Afghan, Deccani and Maratha officers, illustrating how ethnic diversity secured loyalty across far-flung provinces.
  3. 17th century · Maritime boom
    Throughout the 17th century, port cities such as Surat, Hooghly and Masulipatnam exported textiles, indigo and saltpetre to Dutch and English companies, drawing in Iberian and Japanese silver that monetised inland markets.
  4. 1650s – 1700s · Agrarian strain
    Between the 1650s and 1700s, jagir shortages, zamindar revolts (Satnamis, Sikhs) and peasant tax resistance in the Doab exposed how continuous warfare during Shah Jahan and Aurangzeb strained agrarian society.
  5. Early 18th century · Regionalisation
    In the early 18th century, governors like Murshid Quli Khan in Bengal, Saadat Khan in Awadh and Nizam-ul-Mulk in Hyderabad asserted autonomy while retaining Mughal symbols, marking the transition to successor states.

Administrative Fabric

  • Empire divided into subah → sarkar → pargana → village; record keepers (qanungo, patwari) crucial.
  • Jagirdars collect revenue (malgujari) and maintain troops; khalisa lands remit directly to imperial treasury.
  • Mir bakhshi controls appointments/pay; mir saman manages karkhanas.

Societal Layers

  • Nobility, zamindars, peasants, artisans, merchants, religious elites (ulema, pandits, Sufi pirs).
  • Women (Nur Jahan, Jahanara) influence politics, charity, architecture.
  • Bhakti, Sufi, Sikh, Jain, Parsi communities contribute to pluralistic milieu.

Agrarian Economy

  • Zabt system uses uniform bigha, dastur-ul-amal rates; crops include rice, wheat, cotton, indigo, sugarcane.
  • Irrigation via wells, Persian wheels, canals; community labour for maintenance.
  • Zamindars as intermediaries provide troops, maintain local order, sponsor temples/dargahs.

Trade & Urbanism

  • Ports—Surat, Cambay, Hooghly, Masulipatnam—handle textile, indigo, saltpetre exports.
  • Grand Trunk Road connects Agra, Delhi, Lahore, Patna; sarais support caravans.
  • Bankers issue hundis; merchant communities (Baniyas, Bohras, Chettiars) finance long-distance trade.

Cultural Life

  • Miniature schools (Mughal, Rajput, Deccani) flourish; music (Dhrupad, Khayal) matures in courts.
  • Provincial architecture (Lucknow, Murshidabad, Hyderabad) blends local idioms.
  • Literature in Persian, Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi (Rahim, Raskhan, Bulleh Shah) reflects syncretic ethos.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Quote Ain-i-Akbari data for agrarian/administrative answers.
  • Differentiate jagirdar, zamindar, mansabdar roles.
  • Cite European factory records for trade statistics.
Critic's Calibration: Note regional diversity and rising nawabi autonomy—Mughal order stayed adaptive.
  • Covered agrarian, urban and cultural spheres.
  • Linked nobility composition to governance.
  • Flagged proto-regional autonomy in the early 18th century.

Lesson 22 · The Marathas: Shivaji to Peshwas

Timeframe · 17th – 18th centuries CE Shivaji’s fort-centric swarajya matured into a Peshwa-led confederacy whose cavalry dominated the subcontinent until Panipat.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1627 – 1680 · Shivaji
    From 1627 to 1680 CE, Shivaji carved out Swarajya from Pune’s hill forts, raided Surat, crowned himself Chhatrapati at Raigarh and created the Ashta Pradhan council to institutionalise Maratha governance.
  2. 1665 – 1666 · Purandar & Agra
    In 1665–1666 CE, Shivaji signed the Treaty of Purandar with Mughal general Jai Singh, later staged a daring escape from Aurangzeb’s Agra court, showcasing guerilla ingenuity in imperial negotiations.
  3. 1689 – 1707 · Rajaram & Tarabai
    Between 1689 and 1707 CE, Shivaji’s successors Rajaram and Tarabai led resistance from forts like Jinji and Satara, mobilising Maratha sardars during Aurangzeb’s prolonged Deccan sieges.
  4. 1708 – 1740 · Balaji Vishwanath & Baji Rao I
    From 1708 to 1740 CE, Peshwas Balaji Vishwanath and Baji Rao I centralised authority at Pune, collected chauth in Malwa and Bundelkhand, and rode cavalry columns up to Delhi, extending Maratha influence.
  5. 1761 · Third Battle of Panipat (14 January 1761)
    On 14 January 1761 near Panipat, Ahmad Shah Abdali’s Durrani army and Rohilla allies decimated the Maratha confederacy under Sadashiv Rao Bhau, halting northward expansion and leaving a leadership vacuum.
  6. Late 18th century · Confederacy phase
    In the late 18th century, semi-autonomous Maratha houses—Scindias (Gwalior), Holkars (Indore), Bhonsles (Nagpur) and Gaekwads (Baroda)—balanced Peshwa supremacy with British diplomacy, shaping a confederate structure.

Governance

  • Ashta Pradhan ministers (Peshwa, Amatya, Sachiv, Mantri, Senapati, Sumant, Panditrao, Nyayadhish) manage administration.
  • Revenue mix: Chauth (25%) + Sardeshmukhi (10%) plus ryotwari-style village assessments.
  • Fort grid (>300) anchors administration; village sabhas handle local justice.

Military

  • Ganimis kava guerrilla tactics emphasise speed and terrain knowledge.
  • Infantry (Mavalas) hold forts; navy (Kanhoji Angre) secures Konkan.
  • Baji Rao I’s cavalry thrusts reach Delhi, Malwa, Bundelkhand swiftly.

Peshwa Era

  • Balaji Vishwanath secures Mughal farmans legitimising Maratha levies.
  • Balaji Baji Rao (Nana Saheb) presides over zenith but faces Panipat disaster.
  • Madhav Rao I restores stability; later Peshwas confront British diplomacy.

Society & Economy

  • Sardars rewarded with saranjam jagirs; Warkari Bhakti fosters cohesion.
  • Pune’s Shaniwar Wada symbolises urban planning; artisans and bankers thrive.
  • Heavy levies in Malwa/Doab occasionally trigger peasant unrest.

External Relations

  • Conflicts with Mughals, Siddis, Portuguese, Mysore, British.
  • Alliances with Rajputs, Jats, Sikhs remain tactical.
  • Panipat (1761) weakens Marathas, opening space for British ascendancy.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Chauth vs Sardeshmukhi; recall Treaty of Purandar (1665).
  • Quote Shivaji’s titles (Chhatrapati, Haindava Dharmoddharak) for ideology questions.
  • Link Panipat III to later Anglo-Maratha wars.
Critic's Calibration: Focus on institutions (Ashta Pradhan, Peshwa, saranjam) beyond charismatic leaders.
  • Integrated fiscal, military and diplomatic levers.
  • Extended narrative to confederacy politics.
  • Flagged Panipat as strategic inflection.

Lesson 23 · Coming of the Europeans

Timeframe · 1498 – 1765 CE Portuguese, Dutch, English, French and Danish companies reordered Indian Ocean commerce, leading to British Diwani rights.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1498 · Vasco da Gama
    In May 1498, Portuguese captain Vasco da Gama sailed around the Cape of Good Hope to reach Calicut on the Malabar Coast, opening a direct Lisbon-to-India sea route that bypassed Arab intermediaries and reoriented spice trade geography.
  2. 1509 – 1515 · Albuquerque
    Between 1509 and 1515, Governor Afonso de Albuquerque seized Goa, Malacca and Hormuz, imposed the cartaz pass system on Indian Ocean shipping and established fortified factory towns, demonstrating how Portugal sought naval monopoly.
  3. 1602 – 1650s · Dutch VOC
    From 1602 through the 1650s, the Dutch VOC headquartered at Pulicat and later Nagapattinam, ousted the Portuguese from the spice islands, and monopolised nutmeg and cloves while purchasing Coromandel textiles for intra-Asian trade.
  4. 1600 – 1757 · English East India Company
    Between 1600 and 1757, the English Company built fortified bases at Surat, Madras, Bombay and Calcutta, secured Mughal farmans for duty-free trade and after Plassey shifted from commerce to territorial ambition in Bengal.
  5. 1664 – 1761 · French Compagnie
    From 1664 to 1761, the French Compagnie des Indes developed enclaves at Pondicherry and Chandernagore, intervened in Carnatic succession disputes and under Dupleix challenged British influence until defeat at Wandiwash.
  6. 1757 – 1765 · British ascendancy
    Between 1757 and 1765, Robert Clive’s victories at Plassey and Buxar and the Treaty of Allahabad granted the Company Diwani rights in Bengal, Bihar and Orissa, marking when revenue collection funded imperial expansion.

Portuguese Phase

  • Goa, Diu, Daman fortresses; Jesuit missions (St. Francis Xavier).
  • Cartaz pass system controls shipping; monopoly over horses/artillery.
  • Decline as Dutch/English naval power rises.

Dutch & Danes

  • Dutch trade textiles/spices via Pulicat, Masulipatnam, Nagapattinam.
  • Danes operate Tranquebar, Serampore; later sell holdings to British.
  • Primarily commercial aims, limited territorial ambitions.

British Expansion

  • Fortified factories (Fort St. George, Fort William, Bombay Castle) secure merchandise.
  • Farmans (Jahangir 1618, Farrukhsiyar 1717) grant customs exemptions.
  • Clive’s victories at Arcot, Plassey, Buxar shift company from trade to governance.

French Contest

  • Dupleix exploits Carnatic succession disputes; Bussy shapes Deccan politics.
  • Lally’s defeat at Wandiwash (1760) ends French challenge.
  • Treaty of Paris (1763) leaves enclaves but no imperial leverage.

Economic Impact

  • Europeans displace Arab intermediaries; Indian textiles, indigo, saltpetre feed global demand.
  • Bullion inflows sustain monetisation until British political control reverses flows.
  • Cartaz/farman privileges reshape coastal politics and revenue streams.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Memorise battles: Diu (1509), Swally (1612), Wandiwash (1760), Plassey (1757), Buxar (1764).
  • Differentiate motives—Portuguese monopoly, Dutch commerce, British/French politico-military expansion.
  • Cite Mughal farmans granting duty-free trade to EIC.
Critic's Calibration: Emphasise Indian agency—coastal rulers and merchants leveraged European rivalries until mid-18th century.
  • Sequenced European entrants with signature strategies.
  • Linked cartaz/farman privileges to political outcomes.
  • Tied Carnatic wars to British ascendancy.

Lesson 24 · India under the English East India Company

Timeframe · 1757 – 1785 CE Warren Hastings moved the Company from mercantile chaos to territorial governance through fiscal surgery, judicial codification and tightly managed warfare.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1772 · Hastings takes charge
    In 1772, Warren Hastings assumed governorship at Calcutta, abolished Clive’s Dual System, moved the treasury from Murshidabad and created a Board of Revenue so Company officials, not Nawabi agents, collected Bengal taxes.
  2. 1773 · Regulating Act of 1773 (10 June 1773)
    The Regulating Act of 1773, passed in London and implemented in Calcutta, created the office of Governor-General with a council and a Supreme Court, placing the Company’s Indian politics under parliamentary oversight.
  3. 1774 – 1775 · Rohilla War
    In 1774–1775, Hastings loaned troops to Awadh’s Shuja-ud-Daula to expel the Rohillas from Rohilkhand, an alliance battle fought along the Ganga that later formed part of impeachment charges over mercenary motives.
  4. 1775 – 1782 · First Anglo-Maratha War
    From 1775 to 1782, British forces from Bombay intervened in the Peshwa succession, signed and revoked the Treaties of Surat and Purandar, and ultimately concluded the Treaty of Salbai, illustrating why the Company learned to negotiate Maratha politics.
  5. 1780 – 1784 · Second Anglo-Mysore War
    During 1780–1784, Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan coordinated with the French to fight Company armies across Karnataka, culminating in the Treaty of Mangalore that restored territories but exposed British military vulnerabilities.
  6. 1784 – 1785 · Pitt’s India Act & Exit
    In 1784–1785, Pitt’s India Act created a London Board of Control supervising the Company, and Warren Hastings resigned and returned to England to face impeachment, marking how metropolitan scrutiny reshaped colonial governance.

Revenue Reset

  • Dual System scrapped; Company collectors lease districts on five-year settlements monitored by the Calcutta Board of Revenue.
  • Zamindars retain auction priority but face stricter remittance schedules and curbs on arbitrary cesses.
  • Nawab of Awadh’s stipend halved and Mughal tribute discontinued to stem Bengal’s deficits.

Judicial Architecture

  • District diwani (civil) courts placed under European collectors; nizamat (criminal) courts under Indian qazis with Company supervision.
  • Sadar Diwani and Sadar Nizamat Adalats in Calcutta sit atop the hierarchy, aided by pandits and maulvis.
  • Halhed’s Code of Hindu Law and Persian digests standardise procedure; mutilation and ordeal punishments discouraged.

Trade & Administration

  • Dastaks (private duty-free passes) abolished; a flat 2.5 % inland customs duty applied to all merchants.
  • Calcutta named Company capital (1772); Accountant-General, postal system and General Bank of Bengal set up.
  • Thana-based police under Indian darogas organised to curb dacoity.

War & Diplomacy

  • Supports Shuja-ud-Daula against the Rohillas, inviting charges of mercenary warfare.
  • Repudiates Bombay’s Treaty of Surat, concludes Purandar (1776) and Salbai (1782) to stabilise Maratha politics.
  • Treaty of Mangalore (1784) ends the Hyder–Tipu challenge but exposes British military strain.

Constitutional Milestones

  • Regulating Act 1773 makes the Governor-General primus inter pares and binds Company decisions to Parliament.
  • Calcutta Supreme Court (1774) brings English law, sparking Council-versus-Court friction.
  • Pitt’s India Act 1784 creates the Board of Control, splitting commercial and political supervision.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Memorise sequence: Regulating Act (1773) → Act of Settlement (1781) → Pitt’s Act (1784).
  • Differentiate Rohilla War vs. First Anglo-Maratha War causes, allies and treaties.
  • Quote abolition of dastaks, Sadar Adalats and the postal/bank reforms for governance answers.
Critic's Calibration: Pair Hastings’ administrative rigour with Rohilla and Begum controversies—UPSC expects reform plus backlash in the same answer.
  • Linked fiscal fix to abolition of the Dual System and stipend cuts.
  • Highlighted Parliament’s control via Regulating and Pitt’s Acts.
  • Connected expansionist wars to later impeachment.

Lesson 25 · Lord Cornwallis (1786–1793)

Timeframe · 1786 – 1793 CE Cornwallis mixed battlefield grit with institutional reform, defeating Tipu’s Mysore while recasting the Company’s civil service, judiciary and police.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1786 · Governor-General with override powers
    In 1786, Parliament amended Pitt’s Act so that incoming Governor-General Lord Cornwallis could override his council from Calcutta, ensuring decisive leadership for impending military reforms.
  2. 1789 · Tripartite alliance
    By 1789, Cornwallis forged a tripartite alliance with the Nizam of Hyderabad and the Marathas after Tipu’s attack on Travancore, aligning southern rivals to contain Mysore.
  3. 1790 · War in Mysore
    In 1790, Governor Medows launched the first British offensive toward Mysore through the Baramahal while Tipu used rapid manoeuvres, prompting Cornwallis to take personal command.
  4. 1791 · Bangalore falls
    In March 1791, Cornwallis besieged and captured Bangalore, but monsoon failures and logistical shortages forced a temporary retreat to supply bases on the Coromandel coast.
  5. 1792 · Treaty of Srirangapatnam
    The 1792 Treaty of Srirangapatnam compelled Tipu to cede half his dominions, surrender two sons as hostages and pay three crore rupees, redrawing the Mysore map in favour of the Company and its allies.
  6. 1793 · Cornwallis Code
    In 1793, Cornwallis promulgated the Cornwallis Code, separating revenue, judicial and commercial branches, implementing Permanent Settlement in Bengal and raising civil servant salaries to curb corruption.

Third Mysore War

  • Phase I: Medows’ advance checked by Tipu’s mobility in the Baramahal.
  • Phase II: Cornwallis leads siege columns from Vellore, capturing Bangalore (1791).
  • Phase III: Combined British-Maratha forces encircle Srirangapatnam (1792).

Treaty & Aftermath

  • Tipu loses Malabar, Baramahal, Dindigul and half his revenues.
  • War indemnity of 3 crores rupees balances Company finances.
  • Hostage princes in Madras symbolise British dominance yet leave Mysore intact.

Civil Service Purge

  • High salaries replace perquisites to curb graft; forbidden private trade policed.
  • Merit-based recruitment lays seeds of the later Indian Civil Service.
  • Commercial, judicial and revenue cadres formally separated.

Justice & Police

  • Sadar Diwani/Nizamat Adalats at Calcutta supervise provincial appellate courts.
  • District courts under European judges; munsifs introduced at the base.
  • Police thanas (~20 sq miles) run by darogas but answerable to district judges.

Revenue & Commerce

  • Permanent Settlement (Lesson 30) fixed zamindari demand but created absentee landlordism.
  • Board of Trade cleaned up advances, ensured fair treatment for weavers.
  • Cost-cutting through abolition of sinecures stabilised Bengal’s budget.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Treaty of Mangalore (1784) vs. Treaty of Srirangapatnam (1792).
  • Remember Cornwallis Code pillars: civil service, judiciary, police.
  • Use hostage episode and war indemnity as evidence of British leverage.
Critic's Calibration: Cornwallis is both the victor of Srirangapatnam and the architect of an elitist zamindari order—acknowledge the duality.
  • Traced all three phases of the Mysore campaign.
  • Covered civil/judicial reforms beyond just Permanent Settlement.
  • Flagged how aristocratic leadership shaped Company ethos.

Lesson 26 · The Marquess of Wellesley (1798–1805)

Timeframe · 1798 – 1805 CE Wellesley’s Subsidiary Alliance transformed the Company into India’s paramount power, crushing Tipu, isolating the Marathas and absorbing fiscally weak states.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1798 · Arrival & Hyderabad treaty
    When Richard Wellesley arrived in 1798, he forced the Nizam of Hyderabad to disband his French corps and accept a larger British-subsidised garrison near Secunderabad, inaugurating the aggressive Subsidiary Alliance system.
  2. 1799 · Tanjore & Surat settlements
    In 1799, Wellesley pensioned Serfoji II while annexing Tanjore’s administration and assumed control over Surat after succession disputes, showing how the Company took over revenue-rich ports under ‘misrule’ clauses.
  3. 1799 · Fourth Mysore War
    Also in 1799, converging British columns from Bombay and Madras stormed Srirangapatnam; Tipu Sultan died defending the breach, ending Mysore’s resistance and restoring a Wodeyar puppet under subsidiary terms.
  4. 1801 · Carnatic annexed
    By 1801, Wellesley compelled Nawab Azim-ud-Daula to cede the Carnatic’s civil and military administration to the Company, citing debts and misrule, bringing Tamil Nadu under direct British control.
  5. 1802 · Treaty of Bassein (31 December 1802)
    In 1802, the Treaty of Bassein bound Peshwa Baji Rao II to station British troops near Pune, accept a British Resident and renounce external diplomacy, splitting the Maratha confederacy.
  6. 1803 · Assaye & Delhi
    During 1803, Arthur Wellesley defeated Scindia and Bhonsle at Assaye and Argaon, while General Lake captured Delhi and placed the Mughal emperor under Company protection, marking decisive victories in the Second Anglo-Maratha War.

Subsidiary Blueprint

  • Protected rulers host British troops, fund them via cash or territory and forgo independent diplomacy.
  • Rulers must disband native armies and employ no Europeans without British consent.
  • Paramountcy promised non-interference yet created leverage for annexation on “misrule”.

Implementation

  • Hyderabad (1798, 1800), Oudh, Tanjore, Surat, and the Carnatic absorbed heavy subsidiary costs.
  • Serfoji and Surat Nawab retained titles but lost administration; pensions placated elites.
  • Oudh ceded Rohilkhand, lower Doab and Gorakhpur, inviting British interference.

Fourth Anglo-Mysore War

  • Tipu’s French correspondence and diplomacy triggered Wellesley’s pre-emptive strike.
  • Bombay army advanced from Malabar; Madras army under Arthur Wellesley from the east.
  • After Tipu’s death, core Mysore restored to the Wodeyars under a subsidiary treaty.

Second Anglo-Maratha War

  • Treaty of Bassein alienated Scindia, Bhonsle and Holkar.
  • Assaye, Argaon and Laswari (1803) broke Maratha military power; Treaty of Deogaon and Surji-Anjangaon ceded vast tracts.
  • Holkar held out until 1805, prompting Wellesley’s recall amid war fatigue.

Administration & Cost

  • College of Fort William founded (1800) to train civil servants in Indian languages.
  • Massive armies (100k+) strained Company finances, forcing Parliament to scrutinise expansion.
  • Wellesley styled himself “Bengal Tiger,” justifying forward policy as anti-French security.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Remember differences between subsidiary alliance (Wellesley) and Doctrine of Lapse (Dalhousie).
  • Quote key treaties: Srirangapatnam (1792), Bassein (1802), Deogaon/Surji-Anjangaon (1803).
  • Cite College of Fort William when discussing administrative professionalisation.
Critic's Calibration: Wellesley’s expansion delivered paramountcy but left ballooning debts—note both achievement and backlash.
  • Explained subsidiary logic and its coercive application.
  • Mapped both Mysore and Maratha theatres.
  • Flagged financial/political criticism that led to recall.

Lesson 27 · Lord Hastings (1813–1823)

Timeframe · 1813 – 1823 CE Lord Hastings combined aggressive wars against Gurkhas, Pindaris and Marathas with cautious administrative liberalism in press, education and land policy.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1813 · Charter Act & Arrival
    The Charter Act of 1813 ended the Company’s trade monopoly, and Lord Hastings arrived the same year empowered to raise loans and armies, signalling a shift toward territorial-bureaucratic rule.
  2. 1814 – 1816 · Anglo-Nepal War
    Between 1814 and 1816, Hastings dispatched Ochterlony and Gillespie into the Himalayas, defeating Gurkha expansion and signing the Treaty of Sugauli, which ceded Kumaon, Garhwal and Sikkim protectorates to the Company.
  3. 1816 – 1818 · Pindari Campaign
    From 1816 to 1818, Hastings deployed 113,000 troops in a four-pronged campaign that surrounded and disbanded the Pindari raiders in Malwa, illustrating how combined operations secured Central India.
  4. 1817 – 1819 · Third Anglo-Maratha War
    Between 1817 and 1819, British forces beat down the Peshwa, Holkars and Bhonsles at Khadki, Mahidpur and Ashti; Baji Rao II surrendered and was pensioned at Bithoor, dismantling Maratha sovereignty.
  5. 1817 · Hindu College, Calcutta
    In 1817, Raja Ram Mohan Roy and David Hare founded Hindu College in Calcutta with Hastings’ encouragement, institutionalising state-backed English education for Bengali elites.
  6. 1823 · Departure
    In 1823, Hastings resigned after criticism over war expenditure and a controversial commercial partnership, leaving behind an enlarged Company territory stretching to the Sutlej.

Political Context

  • French threat lingered via Zaman Shah and remnants of Napoleonic diplomacy.
  • Non-intervention under his predecessor left frontier states unstable.
  • Hastings believed only decisive action could secure Bengal’s revenues.

Anglo-Nepal War

  • Caused by Gurkha encroachments into Kumaon and Garhwal.
  • Initial British setbacks reversed by Generals Ochterlony and Gillespie.
  • Treaty of Sugauli ceded Kumaon, Garhwal and Sikkim territories; Gurkhas accept a Resident.

Pindari Suppression

  • Irregular cavalry (under leaders like Cheetu, Karim Khan) raided the Deccan and Bengal.
  • 113,000 troops and 300 guns converged from four directions between 1817-18.
  • By 1824, Pindari menace ended; many settled under supervision.

Downfall of the Marathas

  • Peshwa’s attack on the British Residency (Nov 1817) triggered war.
  • Baji Rao II, Bhonsle and Holkar defeated in Khadki, Sitabuldi, Mahidpur and Ashti.
  • Peshwa pensioned at Bithoor; territories form the Bombay Presidency and Central Provinces.

Administrative Measures

  • Supported Ryotwari experiments of Thomas Munro and Reed in Madras.
  • Encouraged missionary printing—Samachar Darpan (1818) and the Serampore Mission press.
  • Improved roads, postal service and welcomed vernacular press freedoms.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate objectives of Pindari campaign vs. Third Anglo-Maratha War.
  • Treaty of Sugauli terms and boundary implications are frequent prelims fodder.
  • Quote his patronage of Hindu College when discussing early education policy.
Critic's Calibration: Hastings completed Wellesley’s conquests but at great cost; note both strategic gains and the heavy military expenditure questioned in London.
  • Covered Nepal, Pindaris and Marathas—the “triple task”.
  • Mentioned reformist streak (Ryotwari, press, education).
  • Linked campaigns to creation of the Bombay Presidency.

Lesson 28 · Lord William Bentinck (1828–1835)

Timeframe · 1828 – 1835 CE Bentinck’s frugal administration paired social reform—abolition of sati, curbs on thuggee, education policy—with the Charter Act 1833’s centralising thrust.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1828 · Arrives as Governor-General
    When Lord William Bentinck landed in 1828, he was tasked with slashing civil and military expenditure after years of warfare, prompting widespread retrenchment from Calcutta.
  2. 1829 · Sati abolished
    In December 1829, Bentinck issued Regulation XVII outlawing sati across Bengal Presidency after consulting Hindu reformers, making widow-burning a criminal offence enforceable by Company courts.
  3. 1830 · Thuggee suppression begins
    By 1830, Bentinck empowered Captain William Sleeman’s Thuggee Department to map, infiltrate and prosecute strangler gangs along the Vindhyan roads, combining intelligence with new tribunals.
  4. 1831 – 1832 · Mysore & Coorg settlements
    Between 1831 and 1832, misrule in Mysore and a rebellion in Coorg led Bentinck to depose native rulers temporarily and install Company commissioners, illustrating how fiscal disorders triggered annexations.
  5. 1833 · Charter Act & Law Commission
    The 1833 Charter Act ended the Company’s trade functions, opened India to all British subjects and created a Law Commission under Macaulay to draft a uniform penal code, centralising legislation in Calcutta.
  6. 1835 · English Education Resolution
    In 1835, Macaulay’s minute and Bentinck’s resolution directed public funds toward English-medium higher education and Western sciences, sidelining Orientalist institutions to create an anglicised intermediary class.

Fiscal Prudence

  • Closed redundant factories, reduced military allowances and merged offices.
  • Encouraged Indian agency in revenue collection to lower costs.
  • Balanced budgets without new taxes—a stark contrast to expansionist predecessors.

Social Reform

  • Sati abolition (1829) framed as both humanitarian and state authority assertion.
  • Female infanticide and human sacrifice suppressed via district-level surveillance.
  • Supported widow remarriage campaigns led by reformers like Ram Mohan Roy.

Law & Governance

  • Charter Act 1833 centralised legislation in a single all-India council.
  • First Law Commission (Macaulay) began drafting the Indian Penal Code.
  • Civil service opened marginally to Indians via competitive nomination (still limited).

Security & Provinces

  • Anti-thuggee operations created a model detective department.
  • Assumed direct control of Mysore and Coorg to quell unrest; restored order through commissioners.
  • Reorganised Bengal army cantonments to cut costs yet maintain readiness.

Education Policy

  • Debate between Orientalists (H. H. Wilson) and Anglicists (Macaulay) settled in favour of English.
  • Calcutta Medical College (1835) and Delhi College promoted western curricula.
  • State grants shifted towards English textbooks but vernacular schools continued through local efforts.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Quote Regulation XVII for sati abolition questions.
  • Link Charter Act 1833 to creation of the first Law Commission.
  • Remember Sleeman’s role in anti-thuggee drives under Bentinck.
Critic's Calibration: Bentinck’s humanitarian reforms rode on fiscal austerity—mention both compassion and cost-cutting.
  • Highlighted social reforms with legal citations.
  • Connected Charter Act 1833 to administrative centralisation.
  • Noted provincial interventions in Mysore/Coorg.

Lesson 29 · Lord Dalhousie (1848–1856)

Timeframe · 1848 – 1856 CE Dalhousie pushed unabashed imperial expansion—annexations via doctrine of lapse and conquest—while ushering in railways, telegraphs and modern public works.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1848 · Dalhousie takes office
    In 1848, Lord Dalhousie assumed office with a mandate to pursue territorial consolidation and administrative modernisation, eyeing lapse, misrule and strategic warfare as tools from his Calcutta council.
  2. 1848 – 1849 · Second Anglo-Sikh War
    Between 1848 and 1849, Dalhousie oversaw the Second Anglo-Sikh War triggered at Multan; victories at Chillianwala and Gujarat led to annexation of the Punjab and application of the Doctrine of Lapse to Satara.
  3. 1852 · Second Burmese War
    In 1852, British forces captured Lower Burma’s Pegu province after disputes over Rangoon customs, giving Calcutta control over teak forests and the Irrawaddy delta’s rice exports.
  4. 1853 · Railways & Telegraphs
    In 1853, Dalhousie sanctioned India’s first passenger railway from Bombay to Thane and authorised a 3,500-km electric telegraph network, revealing how infrastructure served military and commercial mobility.
  5. 1854 · Doctrine of Lapse peaks
    By 1854, Dalhousie invoked lapse to absorb Jhansi and Nagpur when rulers died without natural heirs, asserting that adoption required Company approval and alarming princely elites.
  6. 1856 · Awadh annexed
    In 1856, citing maladministration, Dalhousie deposed Nawab Wajid Ali Shah and annexed Awadh, stationing troops at Lucknow and alienating both taluqdars and sepoys on the eve of 1857.

Doctrine of Lapse

  • Denied recognition to adopted heirs without Company consent.
  • Applied to Satara, Jaitpur, Sambalpur, Jhansi and Nagpur.
  • Seen as legalistic aggression—fuelled resentment among princes.

Annexations & Wars

  • Punjab annexed after Chilianwala/Gujarat battles; Board of Administration governs.
  • Second Burmese War secures Rangoon and Lower Burma for teak and trade.
  • Awadh annexation justified by maladministration but alienated taluqdars and sepoys.

Infrastructure Revolution

  • Railway minute (1853) launches trunk lines—Bombay–Thane, Howrah–Hooghly, Madras–Arakkonam.
  • Electric telegraph stretches from Calcutta to Peshawar.
  • Uniform postal system (1854) with standard rates boosts communication.

Administrative Overhaul

  • Created Public Works Department for roads, canals (Ganges Canal, Bari Doab).
  • Opened civil services to open competition (Charter Act 1853 follow-through).
  • Santhal Parganas carved out for tribal administration and revenue clarity.

Social & Educational Measures

  • Supported Wood’s Despatch (1854) for graded education structure.
  • Encouraged female education and aided Vidyasagar’s widow remarriage campaign (Act of 1856).
  • Yet missionary zeal and revenue pressures deepened rural discontent.

  • Differentiate lapse vs. misrule annexations (Satara vs. Awadh).
  • Quote Railway/Telegraph minutes when discussing modern infrastructure.
  • Remember Dalhousie’s role in Wood’s Despatch and postal reforms.

Lesson 30 · Revenue Administration & Economic Policy

Timeframe · 1765 – 1858 CE British land settlements and trade policies extracted agrarian surplus, redirected commerce to Europe and reshaped India’s economy.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1765 · Diwani Rights
    In 1765, the Treaty of Allahabad granted the Company Diwani rights over Bengal, Bihar and Orissa, making Calcutta responsible for tax collection while the Nawab retained nominal criminal justice, a duality that reshaped revenue politics.
  2. 1793 · Permanent Settlement
    Cornwallis’s 1793 Permanent Settlement fixed zamindari dues in perpetuity in Bengal, transferring ownership to landlords around Calcutta and leaving peasants to bear risks of price fluctuations and famine.
  3. 1820 · Ryotwari in Madras
    By the 1820s, Thomas Munro’s ryotwari settlement in Madras Presidency measured fields village by village, taxed cultivator households directly and adjusted assessments periodically to reflect soil quality.
  4. 1833 · Mahalwari blueprint
    Between 1833 and 1843, Holt Mackenzie’s mahalwari plan in the North-Western Provinces grouped villages into estates that collectively guaranteed revenue, keeping headmen (lambardars) accountable to British collectors.
  5. 1834 · Trade liberalisation
    After the 1834 Charter Act, the end of Company trade monopolies encouraged private planters to expand indigo, opium and tea cultivation for export, increasing pressure on Indian peasants and labourers.
  6. 1853 · Railways approved
    Dalhousie’s 1853 railway minute approved trunk lines linking Bombay, Calcutta and Madras presidencies, indicating how the colonial state sought faster troop movement and commodity flows.

Permanent Settlement

  • Zamindars made hereditary landlords with 10/11th share to the state.
  • Encouraged absentee landlordism; peasants faced rack-renting and eviction.
  • Stabilised revenue but ignored price/rainfall fluctuations.

Ryotwari System

  • Applied in Madras/Bombay; individual cultivators registered with state.
  • Assessment revised periodically (20–30 years) based on soil/productivity.
  • High demand plus measurement errors kept ryots perpetually indebted.

Mahalwari Settlements

  • Village estates (mahals) collectively responsible for revenue.
  • Introduced in North-Western Provinces, Punjab and parts of Central India.
  • Allowed revisions but maintained colonial control via village headmen and patwaris.

Trade & Monopolies

  • Monopolies in salt, opium, indigo ensured bullion flow to Company coffers.
  • Free trade policies flooded India with British textiles, crippling artisans.
  • Plantation crops (tea, coffee) expanded with European capital and labour exploitation.

Economic Impact

  • Frequent famines (1770, 1783, 1837) tied to rigid revenue collection.
  • Peasant uprisings—Bhil, Kol, Santhal, Indigo revolts—mirrored agrarian distress.
  • Railways, telegraphs and canals primarily served export circuits.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Compare assessment principles: Permanent (fixed), Ryotwari (individual), Mahalwari (collective).
  • Quote Charter Act 1833 for trade liberalisation context.
  • Use Indigo Revolt (1859) and Deccan Riots (1875) as consequences in essays.
Critic's Calibration: Highlight how fiscal extraction + free trade produced both infrastructural change and agrarian misery.
  • Defined features/impact of all three settlements.
  • Linked revenue policy to plantations, railways and famines.
  • Supplied revolt examples as evidence.

Lesson 31 · Educational and Social Reforms

Timeframe · 1781 – 1857 CE Colonial education policy evolved from supporting oriental institutions to promoting English, while Indian reformers reimagined society.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1781 · Calcutta Madrasa
    In 1781, Governor-General Warren Hastings funded the Calcutta Madrasa to train qadis and munshis in Islamic law so Company courts in Bengal could work with local jurisprudence.
  2. 1784 · Asiatic Society
    Sir William Jones founded the Asiatic Society at Calcutta in 1784 to study Sanskrit manuscripts, inscriptions and natural history, fuelling Orientalist knowledge that shaped colonial policy.
  3. 1813 · Charter Act clause
    The Charter Act of 1813 earmarked one lakh rupees annually for Indian education, obliging the Company’s Calcutta government to support both Oriental and missionary schools.
  4. 1835 · Macaulay Minute
    Thomas Macaulay’s 1835 minute argued from Calcutta that English should be the medium for higher learning, asserting that a class of anglicised Indians was needed to serve Company administration.
  5. 1854 · Wood’s Despatch
    Wood’s Despatch of 1854 laid down a graded education system—vernacular primary schools, Anglo-vernacular high schools and affiliating universities—directing provincial governments from London to expand secular instruction.
  6. 1857 · Universities Act
    In 1857, universities at Calcutta, Bombay and Madras were chartered on the model of London University, examining students from government and missionary colleges and marking the start of modern higher education.

Indigenous Learning

  • Pathshalas, maktabs and gurukulas continued grassroots education.
  • Orientalist patrons funded Sanskrit College (Benaras, 1791) and Calcutta’s Fort William translators.
  • Missionaries documented village school systems (Adam’s Report, 1830s).

Anglicist Turn

  • Macaulay argued for creating “a class of persons Indian in blood, English in taste.”
  • English textbooks, Western sciences and history prioritised over classical languages.
  • Government jobs increasingly tied to English literacy, reshaping social aspirations.

Institution Building

  • Hindu College (1817), Serampore College (1818) and Agra College (1823) pioneered modern curricula.
  • Medical colleges in Calcutta (1835) and Madras (1835) introduced western medicine.
  • Wood’s Despatch promoted vernacular primary schools, district schools and affiliating universities.

Social Reformers

  • Raja Ram Mohan Roy campaigned against sati, child marriage and for women’s education.
  • Young Bengal (Henry Derozio) championed rationalism; Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar pushed widow remarriage.
  • Missionaries (Alexander Duff) combined western science with biblical teaching.

Policy Debates

  • Orientalists emphasised state support for Sanskrit/Arabic learning.
  • Anglicists stressed English for administrative utility.
  • Wood’s Despatch balanced both—vernacular at base, English at apex.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Charter Act 1813 (fund allocation) from Wood’s Despatch (structure).
  • Quote Macaulay’s statement for essays on cultural policy.
  • Remember Vidyasagar’s role in the 1856 Widow Remarriage Act.
Critic's Calibration: Show how Indian reformers leveraged colonial education policy rather than portraying them as passive recipients.
  • Balanced Orientalist vs. Anglicist perspectives.

Lesson 32 · Palayakkarar Rebellion

Timeframe · 1750 – 1805 CE Tamil Nadu’s palayakkarars (polygars) resisted Company expansion through decades of guerrilla warfare culminating in the South Indian Rebellion of 1801.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1755 – 1764 · Puli Thevar’s Confederacy
    Between 1755 and 1764, Puli Thevar led Tirunelveli polygars from Nelkattumsevval in resisting Nawab of Arcot and Company revenue demands, uniting western palayams through guerrilla warfare.
  2. 1781 · British reorganisation
    In 1781, facing arrears, the Nawab of Arcot mortgaged Tirunelveli revenue to the Company, prompting British collectors at Madras to interfere directly in palayam tribute schedules.
  3. 1793 · Veerapandiya Kattabomman defies tribute
    In 1793 at Panchalankurichi, Veerapandiya Kattabomman refused to pay the escalated kist to Collector Jackson, arguing from Tamilakam that British assessments were predatory.
  4. 1799 · Kattabomman executed
    By 1799, after sieges around Kayathar, the British captured and hanged Kattabomman while his brother Oomathurai fled, demonstrating how Company troops dismantled fort-based chieftaincies.
  5. 1801 · South Indian Rebellion
    In 1801, Marudhu Pandyan brothers issued the Tiruchirappalli Proclamation uniting palayakkarars and Mysore fugitives against Company rule, sparking the South Indian (Polygar) Rebellion across Tamil country.
  6. 1805 · Final suppression
    By 1805, the British demolished polygar forts, deported rebel leaders to Penang and converted palayams into zamindaris under Madras Presidency control, ending hereditary military estates.

Why They Revolted

  • Excessive tribute demands by Nawab of Arcot/Company.
  • Loss of hereditary rights and interference in succession.
  • Refusal to accept direct Company supervision of forts.

Key Leaders

  • Puli Thevar, Veerapandiya Kattabomman, Oomathurai and Marudhu Pandiyars.
  • Dheeran Chinnamalai, Kerala Varma and Muthuramalinga Thevar supported later phases.
  • Women like Kuyili and Vellai Nachiyar offered logistical backing.

British Strategy

  • General Macaulay and Colonel Bannerman led systematic sieges on hill forts.
  • Adopted “divide and rule” by rewarding compliant chiefs.
  • Used accurate cartography and artillery to break stone fortifications.

Outcome

  • Forts razed; palayams converted into zamindaris under strict oversight.
  • South Indian Rebellion (1801) foreshadowed later anti-colonial uprisings.
  • Company established direct revenue administration in Tirunelveli, Madurai, Ramanathapuram.

Legacy

  • Patriotic ballads keep Kattabomman and Marudhu brothers alive in Tamil memory.
  • Rebellion highlighted rural military traditions preceding 1857.
  • Sites like Panchalankurichi serve as heritage symbols.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Polygar Wars from later peasant uprisings.
  • Quote the Tiruchirappalli Proclamation (1801) for early nationalist sentiment.
  • Remember Bannerman’s 1799 campaign for map-based questions.
Critic's Calibration: Treat the palayakkarar struggle as proto-nationalist yet rooted in local autonomy—avoid anachronism.
  • Sequenced both Polygar Wars and 1801 rebellion.

Lesson 33 · Vellore Mutiny

Timeframe · 1801 – 1806 CE The Vellore Mutiny, predating 1857 by half a century, exposed sepoy resentments over cultural interference and harsh discipline.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1801 · Carnatic annexation
    In 1801, the Company formally annexed the Carnatic after sidelining Nawab Azim-ud-Daula, imposing new drill, uniforms and tax regimes on the Vellore garrison.
  2. 1805 · Dress code orders
    In 1805, Sir John Cradock ordered Vellore sepoys to shave beards, abandon caste marks and wear a leather-cocked turban resembling European shakos, sparking resentment among Hindu and Muslim ranks.
  3. 10 July 1806 · Mutiny erupts
    At 2 a.m. on 10 July 1806 inside Vellore Fort, sepoys flags bearing Tipu’s emblems, killed 14 British officers and declared Tipu’s son Fateh Hyder their leader, demonstrating coordinated barrack-level revolt.
  4. Same day · Suppression
    Hours later, Colonel Gillespie rode from Arcot with cavalry, stormed Vellore’s gates and executed hundreds of mutineers on the parade ground, crushing the uprising by midday.
  5. August 1806 · Royal Commission
    In August 1806, a Royal Commission held at Madras blamed insensitive orders; Governor William Bentinck and Commander-in-Chief Cradock were recalled to London, showing accountability chains.
  6. 1810 · Policy rollback
    By 1810, the offending dress regulations were withdrawn, Tipu’s family was sent from Vellore to Calcutta’s Fort William, and the Company reexamined how to respect sepoy religious customs.

Immediate Triggers

  • Uniform changes offended both Hindus (tilak shaving) and Muslims (beard ban).
  • New leather cockades suspected to use cow/pig hide.
  • Tipu Sultan’s imprisoned sons allegedly encouraged discontent.

Course of Events

  • At 3 a.m., sepoys killed sleeping officers and hoisted Tipu’s flag.
  • Sister of Tipu reportedly distributed medals and turbans.
  • Gillespie’s cavalry scaled the walls by noon, ending the revolt.

British Response

  • Harsh reprisals—mutineers blown from guns or shot.
  • Dress regulations immediately rescinded.
  • Command changes: Sir John Cradock replaced; Bentinck recalled.

Consequences

  • Highlighted the need to respect religious customs of sepoys.
  • Prompted shift of royal prisoners from Vellore to Bengal.
  • Served as a cautionary tale cited during 1857 inquiries.

Tamil Context

  • Mutiny occurred in the backdrop of palayakkarar suppression.
  • Local troops recruited from recently subdued polygars.
  • Vellore Fort became a symbol of early anti-colonial sentiment.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Differentiate Vellore Mutiny from 1857 in causes and scale.
  • Name the key official punished—Governor William Bentinck (Madras).
  • Remember role of Colonel Gillespie for map or personality questions.
Critic's Calibration: Emphasise cultural grievances rather than only conspiracy theories when writing about Vellore.
  • Sequenced triggers, outbreak and suppression.
  • Linked policy rollback to mutiny lessons.
  • Located event within Tamil politico-military context.

Lesson 34 · The Great Revolt of 1857

Timeframe · 1857 – 1858 CE India’s first widespread uprising blended sepoy mutiny with civil rebellions against annexations, economic drain and cultural aggression.

Chronological Spine

  1. January 1857 · Greased cartridge rumour
    In January 1857 at Dum Dum and Barrackpore, sepoys learned that new Enfield rifle cartridges were allegedly greased with cow and pig fat, raising fears of forced religious defilement and igniting conspiracy talk across cantonments.
  2. 10 May 1857 · Meerut
    On 10 May 1857, after 85 troopers of the 3rd Light Cavalry were shackled for refusing cartridges, their comrades mutinied at Meerut, freed prisoners and marched overnight to Delhi, signalling a wider revolt.
  3. 11 May 1857 · Delhi seized
    On 11 May, mutineers stormed Delhi, appealed to aged Bahadur Shah II in the Red Fort to head their cause, and killed British civilians, transforming the Mughal capital into the rebellion’s symbolic centre.
  4. June 1857 · Kanpur & Lucknow
    In June 1857, Nana Saheb in Kanpur and Begum Hazrat Mahal in Lucknow organised sieges of Company entrenchments, motivated by annexations and pension disputes, demonstrating provincial breadth.
  5. July – September 1857 · Jhansi & Central India
    Between July and September 1857, Rani Lakshmibai defended Jhansi, Tatya Tope manoeuvred through Bundelkhand, and Kunwar Singh rallied Bihar peasants, weaving local grievances into the revolt.
  6. July 1858 · Government of India Act
    By July 1858, after British reconquest culminating at Lucknow and Gwalior, Parliament passed the Government of India Act transferring Company rule to the Crown, promising non-interference in religion and equal legal protection.

Causes

  • Political: Doctrine of Lapse, annexation of Awadh.
  • Economic: Heavy land revenue, artisan decline, peasant indebtedness.
  • Religious/Military: Greased cartridges, missionary zeal, caste affronts.

Leadership

  • Bahadur Shah II (Delhi), Nana Saheb (Kanpur), Laxmibai & Tatya Tope (Jhansi), Kunwar Singh (Bihar), Khan Bahadur Khan (Bareilly).
  • Peasant & tribal support in Awadh, Rohilkhand, Bundelkhand.
  • Sepoy regiments (3rd Light Cavalry, 34th BNI) triggered domino effect.

Course

  • Initial successes: Delhi captured, British retreat from Kanpur/Lucknow residency.
  • Sieges: Delhi retaken (Sept 1857), Lucknow (Nov 1857–Mar 1858), Jhansi (Apr 1858).
  • Guerrilla phase continues under Tatya Tope until April 1859.

British Response

  • Reinforcements from Punjab, Bombay, and Britain; use of Gurkha and Sikh troops.
  • Retributive violence (hanging, blowing from guns) and property confiscations.
  • Policy rethink culminating in Queen’s Proclamation (1858).

Consequences

  • End of Company rule; Secretary of State and Viceroy offices created.
  • Army reorganised—European ratio increased, artillery kept under British control.
  • Princes promised non-interference if loyal; land settlements reviewed.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Always classify causes (political/economic/social/military).
  • Mention regional variations—Punjab, Rajputana largely loyal.
  • Quote Queen Victoria’s Proclamation promises (religious freedom, equal opportunity).
Critic's Calibration: Avoid calling 1857 a full “national war” yet highlight its multi-class participation.

  • Balanced causes with consequences.
  • Noted the historiographical nuance.

Lesson 35 · British India after 1858

Timeframe · 1858 – 1905 CE The Crown’s Raj balanced autocratic centralisation with cautious liberal reforms under viceroys like Lytton, Ripon and Curzon.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1858 · Government of India Act
    In 1858, the Act for the Better Government of India abolished the Company, created the office of Viceroy in Calcutta and a Secretary of State in London, formalising Crown rule after 1857.
  2. 1876 · Lytton becomes Viceroy
    When Lord Lytton arrived in 1876, he hosted a Delhi Durbar proclaiming Queen Victoria Empress even as drought struck, epitomising pomp amid impending famine.
  3. 1877 · Delhi Durbar & Famine
    In January 1877, Lytton’s Durbar coincided with the Great Famine of 1876–78 across Madras and Mysore, highlighting administrative priorities that favoured ceremony over relief.
  4. 1878 · Vernacular Press & Arms Acts
    In 1878, Lytton’s Vernacular Press Act censored Indian-language newspapers and the Arms Act restricted weapon ownership, revealing how the Raj reacted to rising criticism.
  5. 1882 · Ripon’s Local Self-Government Resolution
    Lord Ripon’s 1882 resolution empowered municipalities and district boards with elected Indian members, laying the groundwork for local self-government and cooperation with provincial officials.
  6. 1905 · Curzon partitions Bengal
    In 1905, Curzon divided Bengal into Hindu-majority West Bengal and Muslim-majority Eastern Bengal & Assam from Calcutta, ostensibly for efficiency but triggering the Swadeshi agitation.

Lytton’s Imperialism

  • Framed Vernacular Press Act (1878) and Arms Act (1878).
  • Held Delhi Durbar (1877) while exporting grain during famine.
  • Second Anglo-Afghan War (1878–80) pursued “forward” policy.

Ripon’s Liberalism

  • Repealed Vernacular Press Act (1882).
  • Introduced local self-government (elected municipalities, district boards).
  • Ilbert Bill (1883) attempted judicial equality but diluted after European backlash.

Curzon’s Centralisation

  • Police, universities (1904 Act), and civil service reforms tightened control.
  • Indian Universities Act (1904) emphasised discipline and official domination.
  • Partition of Bengal (1905) justified administratively, perceived as divide-and-rule.

Economic & Social Policy

  • Famine Commissions (after 1878) recommended relief codes.
  • Hunter Commission (1882) reviewed education; Curzon improved irrigation (Punjab Canal Colonies).
  • Railway expansion and telegraphs integrated markets but favoured exports.

Nationalist Response

  • Indian National Association (1876) and Congress (1885) emerged under Ripon’s liberal atmosphere.
  • Lytton’s repressive acts and Curzon’s partition radicalised politics.
  • Press, theatre and literature critiqued imperial policies.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Match viceroys to policies: Lytton–Arms Act, Ripon–Local Self Gov, Curzon–Partition.
  • Quote Ilbert Bill controversy for questions on racial discrimination.
  • Remember famine codes (Provisional 1883) when analysing colonial welfare.
Critic's Calibration: Juxtapose repression (Lytton/Curzon) with Ripon’s liberal window to explain Congress birth.

Lesson 36 · Socio-Religious Reform Movements

Timeframe · 1820 – 1910 CE Reformers negotiated modernity by reinterpreting scriptures, challenging caste/gender hierarchies and responding to missionary critiques.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1828 · Brahmo Sabha
    In 1828 Calcutta, Raja Ram Mohan Roy founded the Brahmo Sabha (later Brahmo Samaj) to worship a formless God, oppose sati and campaign for women’s education, blending Enlightenment ideas with Upanishadic monism.
  2. 1836 · Young Bengal
    In 1836, Henry Derozio’s Young Bengal movement at Hindu College urged students to question caste and superstition, showing how English-educated youth in Calcutta embraced radical rationalism.
  3. 1863 · Vivekananda’s mentor Ramakrishna
    Ramakrishna Paramahamsa (1836–1886) at Dakshineswar temple experienced ecstatic devotion to multiple deities; his disciple Vivekananda later institutionalised this universalism via the Ramakrishna Mission in 1897.
  4. 1867 · Prarthana Samaj
    In 1867 Bombay, Dadoba Pandurang founded the Prarthana Samaj to promote monotheistic prayer, widow remarriage and social reform, adapting Brahmo ideals to western India’s bhakti idiom.
  5. 1873 · Satyashodhak Samaj
    In 1873 Pune, Jyotirao Phule launched the Satyashodhak Samaj to fight Brahmanical dominance, open schools for girls and Shudras, and reinterpret myth through an anti-caste lens.
  6. 1875 · Arya Samaj
    Swami Dayanand Saraswati created the Arya Samaj in 1875 at Bombay to return Hindus to Vedic purity via shuddhi (reconversion), cow protection and vernacular gurukuls, framing reform as revival.

Brahmo & Prarthana Currents

  • Rejected idol worship; promoted monotheism, widow remarriage and female education.
  • Keshab Chandra Sen’s Brahmo Samaj of India (1866) emphasised universalism.
  • Prarthana Samaj blended bhakti with reform, influencing justice movements in Bombay.

Arya Samaj & Revivalism

  • Dayanand’s “Back to the Vedas” attacked untouchability and child marriage.
  • Established gurukuls and shuddhi (reconversion) campaigns.
  • Later split between reformist and orthodox factions but energised Hindi heartland.

Social Justice Streams

  • Satyashodhak Samaj (Phule) exposed Brahmanical exploitation.
  • Narayana Guru’s SNDP (1903) uplifted Ezhavas; Ayya Vaikundar and Nadar movements fought caste restrictions.
  • Temple entry, inter-dining and education became reform tools.

Muslim Reform

  • Sir Syed Ahmad Khan’s Aligarh movement promoted western education.
  • Deoband (1866) stressed orthodox theology with anti-colonial undertones.
  • Ahmadiyya and Ahl-i-Hadith offered other interpretations.

Spiritual Universalism

  • Ramakrishna Mission (1897) combined Vedanta with service.
  • Theosophical Society (Adyar, 1886) promoted eastern wisdom globally.
  • Revivalists inspired confidence preceding nationalist mobilisation.

  • Group movements by region: Bengal (Brahmo), Bombay (Prarthana, Phule), Punjab/Uttar Pradesh (Arya).
  • Quote key publications: Samvad Kaumudi, Tattvabodhini Patrika, Satyarth Prakash.
  • Mention women leaders—Pandita Ramabai, Sister Nivedita—in essays.

Lesson 37 · Indian National Movement (1885–1905)

Timeframe · 1885 – 1905 CE The moderate phase of the INC built an all-India platform, articulated economic critique and demanded constitutional reforms through petitions and persuasion.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1885 · Bombay Session
    At the Bombay session of December 1885, A.O. Hume convened 72 delegates and W.C. Bonnerjee presided, launching the Indian National Congress as a moderate platform to petition the British for civil rights.
  2. 1886 · Calcutta Session
    In 1886 at Calcutta’s Town Hall, Dadabhai Naoroji presided over the Congress, broadening membership to include more provincial associations and emphasising economic grievances to the Raj.
  3. 1892 · Indian Councils Act
    The 1892 Indian Councils Act slightly expanded and indirectly elected members to provincial and central legislative councils, validating Congress petitions yet revealing British reluctance to share real power.
  4. 1893 · Economic Drain Theory
    In 1893, Dadabhai Naoroji articulated the ‘drain of wealth’ theory in his book Poverty and Un-British Rule in India, arguing in London and Bombay that British remittances impoverished Indian taxpayers.
  5. 1897 · Plague & Tilak trials
    During the 1897 Pune plague, Bal Gangadhar Tilak criticised heavy-handed plague measures in the Kesari, was tried for sedition and imprisoned in Mandalay, illustrating the limits of moderate politics.
  6. 1905 · Partition of Bengal
    When Curzon partitioned Bengal in 1905, Congress moderates saw their constitutional approach failing, catalysing the swadeshi call for boycott and national schools before the extremist split.

Moderate Ideology

  • Faith in British justice; demanded representative institutions.
  • Methods: petitions, resolutions, deputations to Britain.
  • Leaders: Naoroji, Surendranath Banerjea, Pherozeshah Mehta, Ananda Charlu.

Economic Critique

  • Drain theory highlighted unrequited exports (Home Charges, pensions).
  • Demanded reduction of military expenditure and land revenue.
  • Advocated for protective tariffs and textile industry revival.

Organisational Spread

  • Provincial associations (Madras Mahajan Sabha, Poona Sarvajanik Sabha) fed delegates.
  • Annual sessions rotated: Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, Allahabad, Lahore.
  • Congress prayer halls, vernacular pamphlets built political vocabulary.

Limitations & Criticism

  • Leadership dominated by urban elites; peasants/workers indirectly represented.
  • Slow pace frustrated younger leaders (Tilak, Bipin Pal, Lajpat Rai).
  • Loyalist approach ignored colonial autocracy, leading to eventual split.

Tamil Nadu Angle

  • Salem’s G. Subramania Iyer and Chennai’s The Hindu championed Congress causes.
  • Madras session (1898) emphasised civil services parity.
  • Early nationalist press cultivated south Indian political consciousness.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Pair acts with demands: 1892 Councils Act (moderate success), 1905 partition (failure).
  • Cite Naoroji’s drain theory statistics.
  • Know key associations feeding the INC (Poona Sarvajanik Sabha, Indian Association).
Critic's Calibration: Moderates laid intellectual groundwork even if immediate gains were modest—state this balance.
  • Covered ideology, methods, critique and regional spread.

Lesson 38 · Indian National Movement (1905–1916)

Timeframe · 1905 – 1916 CE Swadeshi, boycott and militant nationalism challenged colonial authority, while constitutional reforms offered limited concessions.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1905 · Partition of Bengal
    The 1905 partition carved Eastern Bengal and Assam from Calcutta, prompting mass protests, bonfires of Lancashire cloth and the adoption of the song Vande Mataram as a nationalist anthem.
  2. 1905 – 1908 · Swadeshi & Boycott
    Between 1905 and 1908, activists from Bengal to Bombay organised picketing of foreign goods, set up national schools like Bengal National College and promoted indigenous industries such as Bengal Chemical.
  3. 1907 · Surat Split
    At the 1907 Surat session, disputes over the presidency led moderates and extremists to split; the British deported Tilak in 1908, weakening the swadeshi upsurge.
  4. 1908 · Revolutionary upsurge
    In 1908, groups like Anushilan Samiti and Abhinav Bharat plotted bomb attacks (e.g., Alipore case) and targeted officials, showing how underground cells complemented mass boycott.
  5. 1909 · Morley-Minto Reforms
    The 1909 Morley-Minto Reforms enlarged councils and introduced separate Muslim electorates, granting limited representation while institutionalising communal politics from Simla to London.
  6. 1915 · Gandhi returns to India
    In January 1915, Mohandas Gandhi returned from South Africa to Bombay, toured Champaran and Kheda, and prepared to merge satyagraha with the Congress, foreshadowing a shift to mass politics.

Swadeshi Arsenal

  • Boycott of Manchester cloth, Lancashire salt and foreign sugar.
  • Promotion of indigenous enterprises—Bengal Chemical, National Bank.
  • National education: Bengal National College, Dawn Society.

Extremist Leadership

  • Lal-Bal-Pal popularised passive resistance and public festivals.
  • Tilak’s Kesari, Ganesh/ Shivaji festivals mobilised masses.
  • Bipin Chandra Pal emphasised constructive swadeshi and boycott.

Revolutionary Underground

  • Anushilan Samiti (Bengal) and Abhinav Bharat (Maharashtra) organised bombings.
  • Madras, Punjab and Tamil secret societies smuggled arms.
  • Women like Pritilata Waddedar, Margaret Cousins joined later phases.

Constitutional Developments

  • Morley-Minto Act (1909) introduced separate electorates and enlarged councils.
  • Indian Councils allowed limited budget discussion but retained official majority.
  • Annulment of Bengal partition (1911) placated moderates but capital shifted to Delhi.

Social Base

  • Students, lawyers, small traders, women participated in swadeshi.
  • Labour strikes in Bombay and Madras supported boycott calls.
  • Faith-based organisations (Arya Samaj, Sikh Sabhas) lent networks.

Lesson 39 · Indian National Movement (1917–1947)

Timeframe · 1917 – 1947 CE Gandhi’s mass movements, revolutionary currents and constitutional negotiations converged to secure independence.

Chronological Spine

  1. 1917 · Champaran Satyagraha
    In 1917, Gandhi led a satyagraha in Champaran, Bihar, investigating indigo planters’ abuses, forcing the colonial administration in Patna to repeal tinkathia indenture and proving nonviolent protest could win concessions.
  2. 1919 · Rowlatt Act & Jallianwala Bagh
    The 1919 Rowlatt Act extended wartime detention without trial, prompting nationwide hartals; General Dyer’s massacre at Amritsar’s Jallianwala Bagh on 13 April exposed the brutality of martial law.
  3. 1920 – 1922 · Non-Cooperation & Khilafat
    From 1920 to 1922, Congress allied with Muslim Khilafat leaders, boycotted councils, foreign cloth and law courts, and built volunteer corps until the Chauri Chaura violence led Gandhi to suspend the movement.
  4. 1930 – 1934 · Civil Disobedience
    Between 1930 and 1934, Gandhi’s Salt March to Dandi ignited nationwide civil disobedience, prompting Round Table Conferences in London and the Gandhi-Irwin Pact, while satyagrahis faced mass arrests.
  5. 1942 · Quit India Movement
    In August 1942, the Bombay session launched Quit India with a ‘Do or Die’ call; although the British jailed the entire Congress high command, underground networks ran parallel governments in places like Ballia and Satara.
  6. 1947 · Indian Independence Act
    The Indian Independence Act of July 1947, based on Mountbatten’s plan, legally partitioned British India into India and Pakistan on 15 August, transferring power amid communal migrations and princely integration challenges.

Gandhian Campaigns

  • Champaran, Kheda and Ahmedabad (1917–18) built satyagraha credibility.
  • Non-Cooperation (1920) aligned with Khilafat; suspended after Chauri Chaura.
  • Civil Disobedience targeted salt, forest laws and customs duties; Quit India demanded immediate freedom.

Constitutional Negotiations

  • Government of India Act 1935 created provincial autonomy and federation plan.
  • 1937 provincial elections brought Congress ministries in 8 provinces.
  • Cabinet Mission (1946) and Mountbatten Plan (1947) finalised transfer of power.

Revolutionary & Military Streams

  • Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (Kakori 1925, Saunders 1928).
  • Azad Hind Fauj (INA) under Subhas Bose attacked Imphal-Kohima front (1944).
  • Naval mutiny (1946) signalled disaffection within armed forces.

Social Coalitions

  • Peasants (Kisan Sabha, Tebhaga), workers (AITUC), women (All India Women’s Conference) mobilised.
  • Dalit-Bahujan assertion via Ambedkar (Poona Pact 1932) and Periyar’s Self-Respect Movement.
  • North-East, tribal regions (Tana Bhagat, Praja Mandals) joined anti-colonial struggles.

Communal & Partition Dynamics

  • Lucknow Pact (1916) unity gave way to Two-Nation theory (Muslim League under Jinnah).
  • August Offer (1940), Cripps Mission (1942) failed to reconcile Congress-League demands.
  • Direct Action Day (1946) violence accelerated partition decision.

UPSC Pinpointers

  • Link each Gandhian phase to signature tactics (hartals, Dandi March, Quit India parallel governments).
  • Quote key pacts: Poona (1932), Gandhi-Irwin (1931), Lucknow (1916).
  • Mention INA trials and Naval Mutiny when explaining British decision to quit.
